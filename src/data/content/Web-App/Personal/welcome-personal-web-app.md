# Welcome

You're about to build a web app as a personal project. That means no deadline in 36 hours, no judges, no pressure to fake a demo — just you, building something real, at a pace you can actually sustain.

That's a different optimization problem than a hackathon. And it's a harder one in ways most people don't expect.

---

## The Real Challenge of Personal Projects

Hackathons are easy to finish. The deadline forces it.

Personal projects are easy to start and hard to finish. Most of them stall somewhere between "working prototype" and "thing I'd actually show someone." Not because the builder lost interest — but because without external pressure, the scope quietly expands, the standards quietly rise, and the project quietly becomes something too big to ship.

> ** Warning**
> The most common personal project failure isn't technical. It's scope creep combined with perfectionism. You add one more feature before launch. Then one more. The bar for "ready" keeps moving. A year later the project lives on your laptop and nowhere else.

This curriculum is designed to help you avoid that. Every module pushes you toward a shippable, maintainable, thing-you're-proud-of — not an endless build.

---

## What Personal Mode Optimizes For

| Priority | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Shipping something real** | A live project you can show is worth ten that exist only locally |
| **Learning that sticks** | You'll understand decisions you make yourself far better than ones you copy |
| **Simplicity** | Solo developers who over-architect burn out. Simple systems ship. |
| **Low cost** | Your personal project shouldn't cost more than a coffee per month to run |
| **Maintainability** | You'll come back to this codebase in 6 months. It should be readable. |
| **Pride** | Build something you're genuinely happy to put your name on |

This is not a checklist to complete. It's a filter to run every decision through.

## How This Is Different From Hackathon Mode

If you've seen Hackathon mode modules, you'll notice the advice here is different in meaningful ways.

In Hackathon mode, you skip tests, ignore scalability, and cut anything a judge won't see in 5 minutes. In Personal mode, you make the opposite bets — because *you* are the long-term user of this codebase.

**What changes in Personal mode:**

- Tech stack recommendations prioritize learning and maintainability over raw speed
- Architecture decisions are made to last months, not hours
- You'll be asked to make real product decisions, not just ship the fastest path
- Security basics are included — your personal project will have real users eventually
- The curriculum slows down where slowing down prevents expensive mistakes later

---

## Using AI in Personal Mode

You'll be using AI tools throughout — Kontxt, Cursor, Copilot, or similar. In Personal mode, the relationship with AI is different than in a hackathon.

In a hackathon you accept AI output quickly and move on. In Personal mode, you have the time to understand what AI generates — and that understanding is part of the point.

> ** Tip**
> Whenever AI generates something you don't fully understand, treat that as a learning moment, not a green light to paste and move on. The goal is to build a project *and* grow as an engineer. A codebase you don't understand is technical debt you'll pay every time you touch it.

This curriculum will tell you when to trust AI output quickly and when to slow down and review it carefully.

## What to Expect Across the Phases

**Phase 0 — Discovery**
Lock in what you're building and what the actual MVP is. The most important phase. Mistakes here cost the most.

**Phase 1 — Design**
Plan the user experience before writing a line of code. Even solo projects benefit from thinking through flows on paper first.

**Phase 2 — Architecture**
Choose your stack, database, and hosting with full awareness of the tradeoffs. These decisions are hard to undo.

**Phase 3 — Development**
Build the thing. Frontend, backend, data layer. Modules here are practical and opinionated.

**Phase 4 — Production Lite**
Get it running reliably in the real world. Performance, deployment, and the security basics that matter for a real app with real users.

**Phase 5 — Growth Lite**
Analytics, launch, and presenting your work. Because building it was the first half — getting it out there is the second.

## One Thing Before You Start

Answer this honestly before moving to the next module:

**Can you describe your project in two sentences — what it does and who it's for — without using the words "platform", "solution", "seamless", or "AI-powered"?**

If yes, move on. If not, the Idea Definition module is where you figure that out. It's the right place to start.
