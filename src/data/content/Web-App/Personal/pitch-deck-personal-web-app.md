# Pitch Deck

A pitch deck is not a summary of your app. It's a story about why a problem is worth solving at scale, and why you're the right person to solve it.

You've built the proof. Now make the case.

---

## When You Actually Need This

Not every personal project needs a pitch deck. You need one if:

- You're applying to accelerators (Y Combinator, Antler, Entrepreneur First)
- You're seeking co-founders and want to recruit seriously
- You're raising a pre-seed or angel round
- You're pitching an internal tool to leadership at work
- You want to turn this into a company

If you're sharing a side project with friends or posting on Product Hunt, you don't need a pitch deck. You need a good landing page and a clear description.

---

## The One Thing a Pitch Deck Must Do

Make the investor or reader believe three things simultaneously:

1. **This problem is real and large**
2. **This solution is meaningfully better than the status quo**
3. **This team can execute**

Every slide either builds or undermines one of those three beliefs. If a slide does neither, cut it.

---

## The Structure

This is the structure that works. Not because it's creative — because it's clear.

| Slide | Purpose | Target Length |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Title** | Who you are, what you do | One sentence |
| **2. Problem** | Make them feel the pain | 2–3 bullet points |
| **3. Solution** | Show the better world | One clear statement + demo screenshot |
| **4. Why Now** | Why this moment, not 3 years ago | 2–3 specific reasons |
| **5. Market** | How big is the opportunity | TAM / SAM / SOM |
| **6. Product** | How it works | 2–3 screenshots or a short flow |
| **7. Traction** | Evidence it's working | Numbers, users, growth |
| **8. Business Model** | How you make money | Simple, one slide |
| **9. Competition** | How you're different | Positioning matrix or comparison |
| **10. Team** | Why you | Relevant credentials, not full CVs |
| **11. Ask** | What you want | Specific amount and use of funds |

Total: 10–12 slides. Not 20. Not 30.

> [!WARNING]
> Investors read dozens of decks a week. Dense decks with small text get skimmed. If your slide requires more than 15 seconds to understand, redesign it.

## Slide by Slide

### Slide 1 — Title

```
[App Name]
[One sentence: what it does for whom]

[Your name]  ·  [your@email.com]  ·  [yourapp.com]
```

Don't put your logo and nothing else. Don't put a tagline that sounds clever but explains nothing. Say what you do.

---

### Slide 2 — Problem

Be specific. Specific problems feel real. Vague problems feel invented.

```
 "People struggle to manage their time effectively."

 "Freelancers send 4–6 follow-up emails per unpaid invoice.
    30% of invoices over 60 days old are never collected.
    The average freelancer loses $3,200/year to unpaid work."
```

Use data where you have it. Use a specific personal story where you don't. Investors fund solutions to real problems, not theoretical ones.

---

### Slide 3 — Solution

One sentence. Then show it.

```
[App Name] is the first [category] that [specific differentiation].
```

Follow with a single screenshot of the core feature in action. Not a wireframe. Not a concept mockup. The real product, if you have it.

---

### Slide 4 — Why Now

Most good ideas have existed for a decade. The question is why this moment.

Strong "why now" factors:
- A regulation that just changed
- A platform that just became widely adopted
- A cost that just dropped below a key threshold
- A behaviour shift from a macro event
- A new technical capability (AI, a new API, a new device)

```
"Three things changed in the last 18 months that make this possible now:
1. [Specific change] reduced the cost of X by 80%
2. [Platform] reached [milestone] monthly active users
3. [Regulation] now requires companies to [action]"
```

---

### Slide 5 — Market

Investors need to believe this can be a big business. Size the market honestly.

| Term | Definition | How to Calculate |
|---|---|---|
| **TAM** | Total Addressable Market | Everyone who has this problem globally |
| **SAM** | Serviceable Addressable Market | Who you can realistically reach |
| **SOM** | Serviceable Obtainable Market | Who you'll target in year 1–2 |

```
TAM: 50M freelancers globally → $12B problem
SAM: 8M English-speaking freelancers using digital invoicing → $2B
SOM: 100K freelancers in the US earning $50K+ → $50M
```

Bottom-up is more credible than top-down. "1% of a $1T market" impresses nobody. "We need 10,000 users paying $50/month" is something investors can evaluate.

---

### Slide 6 — Product

Show the product working. 2–3 screenshots of the core flow, annotated with what's happening.

Don't show every feature. Show the moment the user gets the value they came for.

If you have a video demo, link it here. Keep it under 90 seconds.

---

### Slide 7 — Traction

This is the slide that separates personal projects from fundable companies. What evidence do you have that people want this?

| Type of Traction | How to Present It |
|---|---|
| Users | "X signups in Y weeks, Z% week-over-week growth" |
| Revenue | "MRR: $X, growing X% month-over-month" |
| Retention | "X% of users return within 7 days" |
| Waitlist | "X people on waitlist before launch" |
| Qualitative | "Letter of intent from [company]" / notable user quotes |

If you have no traction yet, be honest. Show early signal: waitlist signups, beta users, user interviews, a signed LOI. Something that proves this isn't purely theoretical.

> [!TIP]
> A growth chart that goes up and to the right — even from a small base — is more compelling than a large static number. Show the trajectory, not just the snapshot.

---

### Slide 8 — Business Model

How do you make money? Keep this simple.

```
Freemium SaaS
Free: up to 5 projects
Pro: $12/month — unlimited projects, team collaboration, priority support
Enterprise: $99/month — SSO, audit logs, dedicated support

Current: 80% free / 20% Pro
Target: 15% Pro conversion at scale
```

If you don't have a business model yet, say so and explain what you're optimising for instead (growth, data, partnerships).

---

### Slide 9 — Competition

Never say "we have no competition." Every problem has a status quo solution.

A positioning matrix works well here:

```
                    High Automation
                          |
    [Competitor A]        |        [You]
                          |
Simple ——————————————————————————————— Enterprise
                          |
    [Spreadsheets]        |    [Competitor B]
                          |
                    Low Automation
```

Or a comparison table:

| Feature | Competitor A | Competitor B | You |
|---|---|---|---|
| Real-time sync |  |  |  |
| AI-assisted |  |  |  |
| Mobile app |  |  |  |
| Price | $49/mo | $29/mo | $12/mo |

Be honest. Don't list fake checkmarks for features you don't have. Investors will check.

---

### Slide 10 — Team

Why are you the right people to build this?

For a solo personal project turning into a startup, be direct:

```
[Your Name] — Founder
[Relevant credential]
[Relevant credential]
[Link to the product you built]

Looking for: [co-founder with X background / first engineer / first designer]
```

The product itself is your best credential here. You identified a real problem, built a solution, and shipped it. That's founder behaviour.

---

### Slide 11 — The Ask

Be specific. Vague asks get vague responses.

```
Raising: $500K pre-seed
Terms: SAFE, $4M cap, no discount

Use of funds:
  60% — Engineering (2 hires)
  25% — Growth & marketing
  15% — Operations

Milestone this gets us to:
  1,000 paying customers / $12K MRR by month 18
```

## Prompt: Refine a Pitch Slide

```
Copy Prompt
```

```
I'm preparing a pitch deck for [app name], a [one sentence description].

Here is my draft for the [Problem / Solution / Market / Traction] slide:
[paste your current slide content]

My target audience is [seed-stage VCs / angel investors / accelerator / internal leadership].

Review this slide and improve it by:
1. Identifying any claims that are vague, unverifiable, or unconvincing
2. Suggesting more specific or data-driven language
3. Tightening the narrative — cut anything that doesn't build belief
4. Flagging anything an experienced investor would push back on

Rewrite the slide content with your suggested improvements.
Keep it to what fits comfortably on one slide.
```

## Pitch Deck Checklist

**Content**
- [ ] Problem slide uses specific numbers or a concrete story
- [ ] Solution is one clear sentence followed by a real product screenshot
- [ ] "Why Now" has 2–3 specific, verifiable reasons
- [ ] Market sizing is bottom-up, not "1% of a giant market"
- [ ] Traction slide shows real signal — users, revenue, or letters of intent
- [ ] Competition slide is honest and shows genuine differentiation
- [ ] Ask is specific: amount, terms, use of funds, milestone

**Design**
- [ ] Maximum 12 slides
- [ ] Each slide readable in under 15 seconds
- [ ] No slide is just bullet points — use one visual, chart, or screenshot per slide
- [ ] Consistent fonts, colours, and layout throughout
- [ ] Exported as PDF (not a live link for initial sends — avoids version confusion)

**Before Sending**
- [ ] Someone who doesn't know your project read it and understood it without explanation
- [ ] All claims are accurate and you can back them up with data
- [ ] Your email and app URL are on the title slide
- [ ] The deck is under 10MB

---

## A Note on Design

A well-designed deck signals that you care about craft. A poorly designed deck undermines a good idea.

You don't need to be a designer. You need to:
- Use one font family (two weights maximum)
- Use two to three colours maximum
- Keep every slide uncluttered — one idea per slide
- Use real screenshots, not mockups

Tools that produce clean decks with minimal design skill: Pitch, Linear's deck style, or a well-configured Notion template. Avoid the default PowerPoint and Google Slides themes.
