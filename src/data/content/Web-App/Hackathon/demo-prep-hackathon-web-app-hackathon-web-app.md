# Presentation Prep

You can build the best project at the hackathon and still lose to a worse project with a better demo. Judges spend 3-5 minutes with you, often after judging ten other teams that same hour. What they remember is what you showed them and how clearly you said why it matters — not the elegance of your code.

This module is about converting the thing you built into the thing that wins. It's not fluff — a bad demo actively loses points a good build already earned.

---

## The Judging Reality

> **️ Common Mistake**
> Preparing your presentation like a lecture on how the product works. Judges don't need a feature tour. They need to understand, fast: what problem this solves, why it matters, and proof that it actually works — live, not in slides.

Judges are almost always short on time and context-switching between teams constantly. Your presentation's job is to make their scoring decision easy, not to showcase everything you built.

---

## Structure: The 3-Minute Demo

A tight structure beats a clever one. Use this unless your hackathon's format explicitly demands something else.

| Time | Section | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00-0:30 | **The hook** | One sentence: the problem, and why it's real and urgent |
| 0:30-2:00 | **Live demo** | Show the core flow working, end to end, on the actual deployed app |
| 2:00-2:30 | **What's technically interesting** | One thing worth bragging about — briefly |
| 2:30-3:00 | **Close** | What you'd build next, and a confident, specific final line |

> ** Best Practice**
> Write your hook sentence down and say it out loud ten times before demo day. "We built an app that helps people manage tasks" loses to "Every logistics dispatcher we talked to still tracks driver assignments in a shared spreadsheet that breaks the moment two people edit it at once." Specificity beats scope every time.

---

## Live Demo > Slides

> **️ Warning**
> A slide screenshot of your app is a red flag to judges — it reads as "this might not actually work." If your app is deployed and functional, demo it live. Slides are for the hook and the close, not for showing the product.

**Demo script checklist — decide this in advance, don't improvise on stage:**
- The exact click path you'll follow, memorized
- What data is pre-loaded so the demo doesn't start from an empty state
- A fallback plan if wifi fails (a screen recording as backup, ready to play instantly)

> [!TIP]
> Pre-seed your database with realistic demo data before you go on stage. A demo starting from a completely empty app forces you to type/click through setup steps live, burning your limited time on the least interesting part of your product.

## What Judges Actually Score

Most hackathon rubrics cluster around the same four things. Prepare an answer for each, even if it's not explicitly asked:

| Criterion | What to have ready |
|---|---|
| **Problem/impact** | Your one-sentence hook, and who specifically has this problem |
| **Technical execution** | One specific technical decision you made and why (not a list of every library used) |
| **Design/UX** | This is shown, not told — your live demo does this work for you |
| **Completeness/polish** | Confirm the demoed flow has zero visible bugs, even if other parts of the app do |

> **️ Common Mistake**
> Trying to demo every feature you built. A judge remembers one clean flow far better than five half-explained ones. Cut ruthlessly — showing less, well, beats showing more, chaotically.

## Handling the Q&A

Judges almost always ask at least one question. The most common ones, and how to handle them:

- **"What would you build next?"** — Have a real, specific answer (a feature, not "more polish"). This signals you understand your own product's roadmap.
- **"What was the hardest technical part?"** — Pick one real thing and explain it in one sentence a non-technical judge could follow, then offer to go deeper if they want.
- **"How is this different from [existing tool]?"** — Know your competitive angle before you're asked. Not knowing this on the spot is a bigger loss than a mediocre answer to a hard technical question.

> [!TIP]
> If a judge asks something you don't know, "I haven't figured that out yet, here's how I'd approach it" beats bluffing. Judges have seen hundreds of demos — a confident, honest gap reads far better than an obviously improvised answer.

## AI Prompt

Use this to pressure-test your pitch before you're on stage:

```
I'm presenting this hackathon project in 3 minutes to judges.

Problem: [one sentence]
What we built: [2-3 sentences on the core flow]
Tech we're proud of: [one specific technical decision]

Write a tight opening hook (one sentence) and challenge me on:
1. Is my problem statement specific enough, or does it sound like
   it could apply to any app?
2. Am I about to demo too many features for a 3-minute slot?
3. What's the most likely tough question a judge asks, given this
   pitch, and how should I answer it in one sentence?

Keep your feedback blunt and specific — I'd rather fix this now
than find out on stage.
```

> [!TIP]
> Don't ask AI to write your whole pitch script from scratch — it'll sound generic. Use it to stress-test the specific pitch you already know how to give, the same way you'd use a friend as a practice audience.

## Validation Checklist

- [ ] Hook sentence is specific to a real problem, not generic ("helps people do X better")
- [ ] Demo flow is memorized and rehearsed at least 3 times end-to-end
- [ ] Database/app is pre-seeded with realistic demo data, not empty
- [ ] A backup screen recording exists in case live wifi/demo fails
- [ ] You've picked exactly one technical highlight to mention, not a full stack rundown
- [ ] You have a real answer ready for "what's next," "hardest part," and "how is this different"
- [ ] Full run-through has been timed and fits inside the actual slot given
