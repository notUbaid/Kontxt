# Business Definition

Your store concept is locked. Now define the business logic underneath it.

This isn't busywork. Judges ask questions. Teammates ask questions. Your AI tools will ask questions. If you haven't thought through your business model, your answers will be vague — and vague answers lose demos.

Spend 15 minutes here. It saves hours of confusion later.

---

## Business Model

For a hackathon e-commerce store, you're almost certainly building one of these:

| Model | How money moves | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct-to-consumer** | Customer buys product → you fulfill | Physical or digital goods with a clear catalog |
| **Marketplace** | Third-party sellers list → buyers purchase → you take a cut | More complex, harder to demo convincingly |
| **Subscription box** | Recurring payment → curated products delivered | Strong story, but harder to build in a hackathon |
| **Digital downloads** | One-time purchase → instant file delivery | Simplest fulfillment, easiest to demo |

> **Hackathon recommendation:** Direct-to-consumer or digital downloads. Both are simple to demo, easy to explain, and judges understand them immediately.

---

## Pricing Logic

You need real prices. Placeholder pricing ("$XX.XX") breaks immersion in a demo.

**Set prices that feel real:**

- Research what similar products actually cost
- Pick a price tier and commit to it: budget, mid-range, or premium
- Premium pricing is easier to defend with good design — judges respond to it

**Price anchoring for demos:**

Show at least one product at a higher price alongside your target product. It makes the target price feel like a better deal without any extra work.

| Tier | What it signals | Example price range |
|---|---|---|
| Budget | Volume, accessibility | $5–$25 |
| Mid-range | Quality + value | $30–$80 |
| Premium | Craft, exclusivity | $90–$300+ |

## Revenue Model (One Slide Worth of Logic)

You don't need a full financial model. You need enough to answer: *"How does this make money?"*

Fill this in:

> We charge **$[price]** per **[unit/subscription/download]**.
> Our target customer buys approximately **[frequency]**.
> Our margin is approximately **[%]** after **[primary cost: shipping / production / platform fees]**.

**Example:**
> We charge $45 per handmade ceramic mug. Our target customer buys 2–3 gifts per year. Our margin is ~60% after production and packaging costs.

This is enough. You don't need more than this for a hackathon.

---

## Fulfillment (What Happens After Someone Buys)

Judges may ask: *"How does the customer actually get the product?"*

Pick your answer now:

- [ ] **Ship it** — physical product, shipped via courier (real or simulated in demo)
- [ ] **Instant download** — digital product delivered automatically post-purchase
- [ ] **Third-party fulfillment** — dropshipping or print-on-demand (e.g. Printful, Printify)
- [ ] **In-person pickup** — local or event-based (niche, but memorable for local themes)

You don't need to build fulfillment. You need to be able to describe it clearly.

## Use AI to Stress-Test Your Business Logic

```
I'm building an e-commerce store for a hackathon with this business model:

- What I'm selling: [product]
- Business model: [direct-to-consumer / digital / subscription]
- Price point: [price]
- Fulfillment: [how it gets to the customer]
- Target margin: [estimated %]

Act as a skeptical investor doing a 2-minute due diligence check. Ask me the 3 most important questions about this business model that I should be able to answer. Then tell me if anything about this model is unclear, unrealistic, or likely to confuse a hackathon judge.
```

> **What to do with the output:**
> Answer all three questions out loud or in writing. If you can't answer one confidently, decide now — don't leave it for the demo.

## Competitive Positioning (30 Seconds)

You need one sentence explaining why someone would buy from your store instead of Amazon, Etsy, or a generic alternative.

This is not the same as your value proposition. It's simpler:

> Unlike **[generic alternative]**, we **[specific differentiator]**.

**Examples:**
> Unlike Etsy, we only sell minimalist desk accessories curated for developers.
> Unlike Amazon, every product ships with a handwritten note and zero plastic packaging.
> Unlike generic stock photo sites, all our icon packs are built specifically for SaaS dashboards.

## Validate Before You Move On

- [ ] Business model selected and explainable in one sentence
- [ ] Real prices set for at least 3–5 products
- [ ] Revenue logic written out (the fill-in template above)
- [ ] Fulfillment method decided
- [ ] Competitive positioning sentence written
- [ ] You can answer "how does this make money?" in under 30 seconds
