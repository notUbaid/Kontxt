# Store Fundamentals

Before you write a single line of code, you need three things locked in:

1. What you're selling
2. Who's buying it
3. Why they'd pick you over anyone else

Judges evaluate stores holistically. A technically impressive store with an incoherent concept loses to a simpler store with a clear, compelling identity.

---

## The Three Decisions

### 1. Product Category

Pick one category. Not two. Not "we sell everything."

Judges respond to focus. A store that sells handmade ceramic mugs is more believable and more memorable than a store that sells "lifestyle products."

**Fast category frameworks for hackathons:**

| Type | Examples | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Niche physical goods | Mechanical keyboards, vintage posters, artisan coffee | Easy to demo, visual, relatable |
| Digital products | Icon packs, resume templates, Notion dashboards | No inventory complexity, instant delivery |
| Curated collection | Sustainable fashion, local artisan goods | Strong story for judges |
| Themed novelty | Space-themed merch, retro gaming gear | Memorable, great visual identity |

> **Hackathon tip:** Pick something with good product imagery available online. Your demo will look significantly better with real, high-quality photos than with placeholders.

---

### 2. Target Customer

One sentence. One person.

❌ "Young people who like fashion"
✅ "Remote developers who want a minimal, distraction-free desk setup"

The more specific your customer, the easier every design and copy decision becomes. You won't be debating fonts — you'll know what your customer expects.

---

### 3. Value Proposition

Why does your store exist? What does it do better, differently, or more specifically than the generic alternative?

**Formula:**
> We sell **[specific product]** for **[specific customer]** who want **[specific outcome]** without **[specific friction]**.

**Example:**
> We sell premium mechanical keyboard accessories for developers who want a clean desk aesthetic without spending hours researching compatibility.

This sentence will become your homepage headline. Write it well now.

---

## Use AI to Pressure-Test Your Concept

Copy this prompt. Paste it into your AI tool of choice. Replace the bracketed sections.

```
I'm building an e-commerce store for a hackathon. Here's my concept:

- Product category: [your category]
- Target customer: [your one-sentence customer]
- Value proposition: [your value proposition sentence]

Act as a skeptical hackathon judge. Identify:
1. The single biggest weakness in this concept
2. Whether the target customer is specific enough
3. Whether this is believable as a real store
4. One way to make the concept sharper or more memorable

Be direct. Don't soften the feedback.
```

> **What to look for in the response:**
> - If AI says your customer is too broad → it's right, narrow it
> - If AI struggles to identify a weakness → your concept is solid
> - If AI suggests a sharper angle → seriously consider it

---

## Validate Before You Move On

- [ ] Product category is a single, specific niche
- [ ] Target customer is one type of person, described in one sentence
- [ ] Value proposition fits the formula and sounds like a real headline
- [ ] You can name 5–8 products you'd actually sell in this store
- [ ] You have a store name (even a working title)

---

## Store Name (Quick Decision)

You need a name before Phase 1. It doesn't have to be perfect — it has to be usable.

**Fast naming heuristics:**
- Short (1–2 words)
- Sounds like a real brand
- Relates to the product, feeling, or customer — not all three
- Available as a `.com` domain (check quickly — you may need it for the demo URL)
