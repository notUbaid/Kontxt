# Presentation Prep

Your store is built. Now you need to sell it.

Technical execution gets you to the finals. Presentation wins them.

Judges evaluate dozens of projects. Most are forgotten within minutes. The ones that stick are the ones with a clear story, a confident demo, and a team that knew exactly what they built and why.

This module gives you the framework to present like you've done this before.

---

## The Anatomy of a Winning Hackathon Pitch

A strong e-commerce pitch has five beats. In order:

```
1. The Problem      — Why does this store need to exist?
2. The Solution     — What did you build?
3. The Demo         — Show it working, end to end
4. The Differentiation — Why is this better than the obvious alternative?
5. The Ask / Close  — What are you leaving judges with?
```

You have 3–5 minutes. Each beat gets roughly 30–60 seconds. The demo gets the most time.

---

## Beat 1: The Problem (30 sec)

Don't open with "we built an e-commerce store." Every team built one.

Open with the customer and their pain.

**Weak opening:**
> "We built an online store that sells handmade ceramics."

**Strong opening:**
> "Finding handmade ceramics online is a nightmare. You're either scrolling through 10,000 Etsy listings with no quality filter, or paying boutique prices for something you can't verify is actually handmade. We built a store that solves exactly that."

One problem. One customer. Thirty seconds.

---

## Beat 2: The Solution (30 sec)

Name what you built, what it does, and who it's for — in two sentences.

**Template:**
> "[Store name] is an online store for [specific customer] who want [specific outcome]. We sell [specific product], with [the differentiating thing that makes your store worth visiting].

Keep it concrete. Don't say "seamless experience" or "intuitive interface." Say what it actually does.

---

## Beat 3: The Demo (90–120 sec)

This is the most important part of your pitch. Everything else supports it.

**The golden demo path:**
1. Start on the homepage — let judges see the brand
2. Browse the catalog — click a product
3. Add it to the cart — show the count update
4. Go to cart — confirm items and subtotal
5. Checkout — fill the form (pre-filled is fine), place the order
6. Land on confirmation — order ID visible, cart cleared

**Demo rules:**

| Rule | Why |
|---|---|
| Pre-fill the checkout form | Watching someone type an address is painful |
| Use a real product image, real product name | Placeholders destroy credibility |
| Have the test card number ready | `4242 4242 4242 4242` — don't look it up live |
| Never apologise mid-demo | "Sorry this is slow" plants doubt. Stay silent or redirect |
| Have a backup screen recording | If live demo fails, play the video without breaking stride |

> **The pre-fill trick:** Add a "Demo Mode" button visible only during the presentation that auto-fills the checkout form. Takes 10 minutes to build, saves the pitch if someone else is driving the keyboard.

---

## Beat 4: The Differentiation (30 sec)

Why would someone buy from your store instead of Amazon or Etsy?

You wrote this sentence in Phase 0. Use it here — refined:

> "Unlike Etsy, we don't make customers sort through thousands of listings. Every product in [store name] is hand-curated by us, photographed consistently, and ships in branded packaging. We're not a marketplace. We're a brand."

One clear differentiator. Delivered with confidence.

---

## Beat 5: The Close (30 sec)

End with something memorable. Options:

| Close type | When to use |
|---|---|
| **The number** | "In 48 hours, we built a store with 8 products, a full checkout flow, and Stripe integration." |
| **The vision** | "This is the MVP. The next step is user accounts and personalised recommendations." |
| **The question flip** | End with: "We're happy to take questions." Then stop. Don't trail off. |

Never end with "that's it" or "yeah, so... that's our project."

## What Judges Are Actually Scoring

Different hackathons use different rubrics, but most e-commerce judges weight:

| Criterion | What they're looking for |
|---|---|
| **Functionality** | Does it actually work? Can you complete a purchase? |
| **Design** | Does it look like a real store? Would someone trust it? |
| **Innovation** | Is there a differentiating idea, or is it a vanilla Shopify clone? |
| **Presentation** | Is the team confident? Is the story clear? |
| **Technical complexity** | Stripe integration, responsive design, data architecture |

> Know your rubric before you present. If judges share scoring criteria, read them and explicitly address each one during your pitch.

## Pre-Presentation Checklist

Run through this the hour before your presentation.

### Store
- [ ] All product pages load without errors
- [ ] Add to Cart works on at least 3 products
- [ ] Cart shows correct quantities and subtotals
- [ ] Checkout form validates correctly
- [ ] Payment flow completes (Stripe or simulated)
- [ ] Order confirmation shows order ID
- [ ] No 404s on any linked page
- [ ] No console errors visible in browser DevTools
- [ ] Images load on all pages (no broken image icons)

### Presentation
- [ ] Demo path rehearsed at least twice
- [ ] Checkout form pre-fill ready (or memorised test data)
- [ ] Test card number memorised or on a sticky: `4242 4242 4242 4242`
- [ ] Backup screen recording exists
- [ ] Each team member knows their speaking role
- [ ] Pitch fits within the time limit (time it)
- [ ] Opening line rehearsed — first 10 seconds are the hardest

### Environment
- [ ] Laptop is plugged in
- [ ] Browser tabs: only your store, nothing embarrassing open
- [ ] Text zoom at 100% (or appropriate for the room)
- [ ] Localhost server running (if not deployed)
- [ ] Deployment URL tested from a different network if deployed

## Use AI to Sharpen Your Pitch

```
I'm presenting an e-commerce hackathon project. Here are my details:

Store name: [name]
Product category: [category]
Target customer: [one sentence]
Key differentiator: [one sentence]
Payment: [Stripe test mode / simulated]
Time built in: [hours]
Team size: [number]

Write a 3-minute hackathon pitch script with these sections:
1. Opening hook (problem statement, 30 sec)
2. Solution statement (30 sec)
3. Demo narration script — what to say while clicking through the store (90 sec)
4. Differentiation statement (30 sec)
5. Closing line (10 sec)

Write it to be spoken aloud, not read. No filler. No corporate language. Sound like a confident founder.
```

> **After you get the script:**
> - [ ] Read it aloud — does it sound natural?
> - [ ] Time it — is it under the limit?
> - [ ] Cut any sentence that doesn't make the project sound better
> - [ ] Memorise the opening and closing lines — everything else can be improvised
