# Idea Definition

🕒 **Estimated Time:** 15 Minutes

---

A good startup idea and a good hackathon idea are not the same thing.

A startup idea needs to be true, defensible, and sustainable over years. You can spend months validating it.

A hackathon idea needs to be demonstrable, understandable, and buildable in a weekend. It still has to be real — but the constraints are different.

The most common failure mode at hackathons is not a broken build or a crashed demo. It is a team that spent 24 hours building something nobody asked for, because they never stopped to ask whether the problem they're solving actually exists.

This topic exists to prevent that.

---

## Before You Decide On Anything, Answer These Three Questions

Your idea needs to survive three filters before you commit to it. If any filter fails, sharpen the idea before continuing.

**Filter 1: Can you name the specific person who has this problem?**

Not "small businesses." Not "content creators." Not "people."

Specific. *Freelance graphic designers who invoice clients manually in Google Docs.* *Restaurant owners who manage shift schedules over WhatsApp.* *Solo founders who lose track of which investors they've already emailed.*

The more specific the person, the sharper every subsequent decision becomes — what features matter, what the UI should prioritize, what the demo should show.

**Filter 2: What does that person do right now, without your product?**

This is the alternative. Your product replaces or improves something that already exists.

If the answer is "nothing, nobody deals with this" — be suspicious. Real pain has real workarounds. People are dealing with it somehow, even if that way is embarrassing. If you can name the workaround (Excel spreadsheets, copying between tabs, doing it manually, hiring someone), you've confirmed the problem is real. The workaround *is* the market validation.

**Filter 3: What will a judge see on screen in 90 seconds that makes them say "oh, that's actually useful"?**

Not what they'll understand. What they'll *see*.

If your demo requires five minutes of context-setting before any value is visible, you don't have a demo. You have a concept. The judges have seen 30 concepts today. They will not give you five minutes.

## The One-Sentence Test

Write this sentence, then read it out loud:

> **[Product] helps [specific person] [do a specific thing] so they can [achieve a specific outcome].**

A concrete example of what this looks like when done right:

> Shiftlog helps café managers build weekly staff schedules so they can stop resolving last-minute swap requests over text.

Notice what that sentence doesn't contain: "AI-powered," "platform," "seamlessly," "next-generation," "leverage." If you need those words to make the idea sound valuable, the underlying idea isn't strong enough yet.

Test your sentence on someone not on your team. If they respond with *"oh, like a..."* and name something they already know — good. That means they understood it. If they say *"wait, so what does it actually do?"* — rewrite the sentence before moving forward.

This one sentence becomes your north star for every decision that follows. Feature doesn't fit in the sentence? Probably not in the MVP. Demo doesn't illustrate the sentence? Fix the demo.

## Vitamin or Painkiller

Every product is either a vitamin or a painkiller.

Vitamins are beneficial but optional. People take them when they remember. They don't feel the absence. They'll cancel the subscription when they're cutting costs.

Painkillers solve something urgent. People notice immediately when the pain comes back. They'll pay before they've fully evaluated the alternatives.

For a hackathon demo, painkillers win. Not because vitamins are bad businesses — they can be excellent businesses — but because painkillers *demo better*. The before and after are obvious without explanation.

A quick test: if someone discovered your product and then couldn't access it for a week, would they be frustrated, or would they just forget about it?

If the honest answer is "forget about it" — that's not automatically fatal, but you should reconsider your positioning. There is usually a version of the same idea that feels more urgent. Find it.

## The SaaS Angle

You are building a SaaS, which means someone is supposed to pay monthly for continued access. That word — *continued* — is the important one.

A one-time tool is not a SaaS. A calculator is not a SaaS. A resume generator that someone uses once and never again is not a SaaS — it's a utility that happens to live on the web.

A SaaS has a reason for someone to still be paying six months after they signed up. That reason is almost always one of these:

- The user's data lives and grows in your product (CRM, logs, notes, analytics)
- The problem they're solving is ongoing and repeating (invoicing, scheduling, monitoring)
- The product is collaborative and the team now depends on it
- The value compounds over time (the product gets more useful the more someone uses it)

For your demo, you don't need to have built all of this. But when a judge asks *"why is this a subscription and not a one-time purchase?"* — and one of them will ask — you need a real answer. Think about it now, not at the pitch.

## What Judges Are Actually Evaluating

Most hackathon judges are practitioners. They have built things. They have seen hundreds of demos. They are not evaluating your code quality. They are evaluating your thinking.

What earns respect from judges who have seen real products:

- A problem statement that makes them nod because they recognize the pain
- A demo that shows the core workflow happening, not a feature list
- Evidence that you know who the specific user is
- A product that feels designed rather than assembled

What signals inexperience:

- "We used AI to..." as if the technology is the product
- A product that should be a Chrome extension being pitched as an enterprise platform
- A feature list with no demonstration of which feature is the actual core value
- A solution where the problem has to be explained at length before anyone cares

None of this means your product needs to be complete. Judges understand hackathon constraints. What they cannot forgive is a team that didn't think clearly about what they were building or why.

## Common Traps

**The "everyone" user.** Targeting "everyone" or "all businesses" means you've built for nobody specific. Scope down. A product that one type of person loves is worth more than a product ten types of people are indifferent to.

**Starting with the technology.** "We want to build something with [new API / trending framework]" is not an idea. It is a technical experiment. Work backwards from a problem, not forwards from a stack.

**The feature-list pitch.** Listing ten things your product can do is not a product vision. It is a changelog. Pick the one thing that is the actual core value, and make sure that one thing is visible in the demo.

**The scope drift.** Ideas that aren't well-defined invite constant negotiation. If your idea is vague, every alternative idea will sound better at 2am when the build is breaking and the team is tired. Lock the idea down now, in writing, so it cannot be relitigated.

**Building what sounds impressive.** "AI-powered personalized recommendations for X" sounds better than "a simple dashboard that tracks X." But if the simple dashboard solves the problem and you can demo it, it will beat the complex AI feature that isn't working by demo time.

## AI Prompts for This Step

Use these after you have a rough idea. Do not use them to generate ideas from scratch. If you don't understand the problem yourself, AI will produce something that sounds coherent but has no foundation — and you will not be able to answer follow-up questions about it at the pitch.

---

**Stress-test your idea**

Use this to expose weak assumptions before you start building. The goal is to find problems now, not during the demo.

```
I'm building a SaaS for a hackathon. Here is the idea:

[Your one-sentence pitch]

Target user: [Describe them specifically — job, context, situation]
What they currently do without this product: [Describe the existing workaround]

Act as a skeptical but fair investor who has seen hundreds of pitches. Ask me the five hardest questions about this idea — the ones that would make a founder squirm. Do not soften them.

After listing all five questions, go back through each one and tell me: (a) how I could answer it well, or (b) how I could adjust the idea so the question no longer applies.
```

---

**Sharpen the one-sentence pitch**

Use this when your description is still messy and you need to find the crisp version.

```
Here is my rough description of the product I'm building:

[Your messy description — don't clean it up first, paste it as-is]

Rewrite this as three distinct one-sentence pitch variations following this exact format:
"[Product] helps [specific person] [do a specific thing] so they can [achieve a specific outcome]."

Rules:
- Each variation should emphasize a slightly different user or outcome
- No buzzwords: do not use "seamlessly," "AI-powered," "platform," "leverage," "streamline," or "next-generation"
- Be specific and plain
- Do not explain the variations — just write the three sentences
```

---

**Map the demo path**

Use this to figure out exactly what judges will see, and in what order.

```
I'm building a SaaS for a hackathon. The core idea is:

[Your one-sentence pitch]

I have 90 seconds to demo this to judges who can see my screen. They are experienced practitioners.

Design the exact demo walkthrough: what screen appears first, what I click or type, what happens on screen after each action, and what I say aloud while it is happening. The goal is to show the core value as early as possible.

At the end, tell me the one thing in this demo that is most likely to impress an experienced judge, and the one thing most likely to fall flat.
```

---

## Reviewing AI Output From These Prompts

AI is useful here, but it has predictable failure modes. Check for these before accepting any output:

**It made the scope bigger.** AI defaults to suggesting additions and expansions. You are in a hackathon. Ignore anything that adds features unless it directly clarifies the core value.

**It used vague language.** If the output contains "streamline workflows," "empower users," or "boost productivity" — those parts are useless. Rewrite them yourself with specific verbs and outcomes.

**The demo path assumes features you haven't built.** AI doesn't know what you can ship in 48 hours. Read the demo path critically. Remove every step that would require more than a few hours to implement.

**The stress-test was too gentle.** If the five hard questions feel easy to answer, push back. Ask AI to be more adversarial. A stress-test that doesn't hurt isn't doing its job.

**The target user got vaguer.** Watch for AI broadening your user from "freelance designers who invoice in Google Docs" to "creative professionals." The broader version sounds more market-sized, but it's actually less useful. Stay specific.

## Your Deliverable

Do not move to MVP Features until you have written and saved all three of these. They will be referenced in the PRD, and they anchor every scoping decision in the next step.

**The Idea Statement**

One sentence. Specific user. Specific action. Specific outcome. No buzzwords.

**The Context Paragraph**

Three to five sentences: what problem exists, who specifically has it, what they do today without your product, and what your product does that the current solution doesn't.

**The Demo Moment**

One paragraph: what a judge will see during your 90-second demo. What is on screen, what you click, what result appears. This does not need to be polished — it just needs to be concrete enough that your whole team agrees on it.

Write these in plain text somewhere your whole team can see. This is your contract with each other about what you are building. If someone suggests a feature later that isn't connected to this document, that is the conversation you have: does this change the Idea Statement, or is it out of scope?
