# Existing Alternatives

Competitor Analysis mapped the products competing in your space. This module covers something different — and something most founders miss entirely.

Your users are not choosing between you and your competitors.

They're choosing between you and whatever they're doing right now.

That might be a competitor. But it's often a spreadsheet, a manual process, a combination of three tools duct-taped together, or simply doing nothing. These are your **existing alternatives** — and they are frequently harder to displace than any named competitor.

---

## Why This Is Its Own Module

In Competitor Analysis you studied products. Here you study behavior.

The distinction matters because users don't switch to better products. They switch when the pain of staying exceeds the cost of changing. Understanding existing alternatives tells you exactly how high that bar is — and whether your product clears it.

If you skip this, you'll build against the wrong enemy, price against the wrong benchmark, and write copy that misses what users actually compare you to when they're deciding.

---

## The Five Categories of Existing Alternatives

Every user solving a problem has at least one of these in place already.

<details>
<summary><strong>1. The Manual Workflow</strong></summary>

The user does this by hand. Spreadsheets, email threads, copy-paste, calendar reminders, sticky notes, shared documents.

**Why it's hard to displace:** It's free, already set up, and "good enough." Users have invested time in their current system. Switching means admitting that time was wasted and learning something new.

**What you're actually selling against:** Inertia and the sunk cost of their existing setup.

**What you need:** A time-to-value fast enough that they see results before they have time to regret switching.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. The Stitched Stack</strong></summary>

The user combines multiple tools to solve the problem — Zapier automations, Notion databases wired to Airtable, Google Sheets feeding into a dashboard, manual exports between systems.

**Why it's hard to displace:** It works. Imperfectly, but it works. The user built it themselves, so they understand every part. And it's often cheaper than a dedicated solution.

**What you're actually selling against:** Familiarity and the fear that your product will be less flexible than their custom setup.

**What you need:** A clear demonstration that your product handles the edge cases their stack breaks on.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>3. The Wrong Tool</strong></summary>

The user is solving this problem with a tool that wasn't designed for it. Using Slack as a CRM. Using Notion as a project tracker. Using Excel as a database. Using Gmail as a support inbox.

**Why it's hard to displace:** The wrong tool is already paid for. The user is already in it every day. Switching means data migration and workflow disruption.

**What you're actually selling against:** The switching cost and the comfort of a familiar environment.

**What you need:** A migration path that's low-friction and a clear before/after story.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>4. The Human Alternative</strong></summary>

The user pays a person to do this. A VA, a contractor, an agency, an intern, a part-time hire.

**Why it's hard to displace:** Humans are flexible. A VA adapts to edge cases your software won't handle. There's also a relationship involved — firing a VA to replace them with software feels different than switching tools.

**What you're actually selling against:** Perceived flexibility and the discomfort of replacing a person with a product.

**What you need:** To demonstrate that your product handles the 90% so their human can focus on the 10% that actually requires judgment.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>5. Doing Nothing</strong></summary>

The user has decided the problem isn't worth solving yet. They know the pain exists. They've accepted it.

**Why it's hard to displace:** There's no switching cost to nothing. But there's also no urgency. You're not competing with a product — you're competing with inaction and deprioritization.

**What you're actually selling against:** The belief that the problem can wait.

**What you need:** A trigger — an event, a threshold, a moment of acute pain — that makes "doing nothing" feel unacceptable. Your go-to-market needs to intercept users at that moment.

</details>

## Map Your Alternatives

For each alternative your target users rely on today, fill this in:

| Alternative | Why They Use It | What Breaks Down | Switching Trigger |
|---|---|---|---|
| [e.g. Google Sheets] | Free, familiar, no setup | Breaks at scale, no automation | When manual updates take >X hours/week |
| [e.g. Notion + Zapier] | Flexible, already set up | Fragile automations, no reporting | When a Zapier error causes a real problem |
| [e.g. VA / contractor] | Handles edge cases | Expensive, slow, inconsistent | When headcount costs become a line item |

The **Switching Trigger** column is the most important. This is the moment your ICP is most likely to buy. Your go-to-market, your copy, and your onboarding should all be designed around intercepting users at that moment.

## The Switching Cost Problem

Even when users want to switch, switching costs stop them. Know which ones apply to your alternatives:

**Data migration** — Their historical data lives in the old system. Moving it is painful or lossy.

**Workflow retraining** — Their team knows how to use the current approach. Retraining takes time they don't have.

**Integration dependencies** — The existing system connects to other tools. Replacing it breaks those connections.

**Psychological investment** — They built it themselves. Abandoning it feels like failure.

**Price anchoring** — The current solution is free or cheap. Any cost feels like a downgrade.

If your target users face multiple high switching costs, you need to either lower those costs directly (e.g., automated migration, free onboarding support) or make your value so obvious that the costs feel worth it. Pretending switching costs don't exist is how you lose pilots.

## Use AI to Pressure-Test Your Assumptions

You likely have a model in your head of what users are doing today. That model is probably incomplete.

```prompt
I'm building a SaaS product for [target user] that solves [specific problem].

My value proposition: [paste it]

I want to understand what my target users are doing today instead of using
a dedicated product like mine.

Help me:

1. List the most likely manual workflows or tool combinations they're using
   to solve this problem right now
2. For each, explain why it works well enough that they haven't switched yet
3. For each, explain what breaks down or frustrates them about it
4. Identify the most common trigger event that would make them look for
   a dedicated solution
5. List the switching costs I need to account for when designing onboarding

Be specific to [target user type] — not generic SaaS advice.
Flag anything you're uncertain about.
```

Cross-reference the output against what you heard in user conversations. If AI suggests alternatives you haven't heard users mention, investigate whether those actually apply to your segment.

## What This Changes About Your Product

Understanding existing alternatives should directly affect decisions you haven't made yet:

**Onboarding design** — If users are migrating from spreadsheets, your onboarding needs an import flow on day one. If they're replacing a VA, they need to see time saved in the first session.

**Pricing** — If the alternative is free (manual process), your price needs to be justifiable against time saved, not feature count. If the alternative is a $3,000/month agency, $200/month is a bargain.

**Copy** — The most effective landing page copy names the alternative explicitly. "Still tracking this in spreadsheets?" converts better than any generic headline because it meets users where they are.

**Go-to-market timing** — If users switch at a specific trigger event, you want to be present at that moment — through SEO, communities, or partnerships — not six months before it.

**Feature prioritization** — The features that matter most are the ones that eliminate the worst parts of the current alternative. Not the features that are impressive in a demo.
