# Elevator Pitch

Your solution statement is precise and complete. It is also too long and too technical to say out loud to someone who has not asked for a deep explanation.

The elevator pitch is what you say in 30–60 seconds when someone asks what you are building. It is the compressed, spoken version of your solution statement — stripped to its essential logic, calibrated for a specific audience, and rehearsed enough to sound natural rather than recited.

It is also the hardest thing to write well, because compression forces clarity. Every vague word that survived in your longer documents becomes obvious when you have 60 seconds to make someone understand and care.

---

## What an Elevator Pitch Must Do

In 60 seconds or less, it must accomplish four things in order.

```
1   Establish the problem          Make them feel the pain
2   Name who has it                Give them someone to identify with
3   Explain your solution          One sentence, mechanism-first
4   Signal why it works            The differentiating insight
```

Notice what is not on this list: your tech stack, your background, your funding status, your roadmap, your team, and every feature except the one that proves the mechanism.

If any of those need to be in the pitch, they go in the conversation that follows — not in the 60 seconds.

---

## The Audience Problem

The same pitch does not work for every audience.

A pitch calibrated for an investor lands differently with a potential user. A pitch that works in a technical community sounds wrong to a business audience.

You need variants. Not entirely different pitches — the core logic stays the same — but adjusted emphasis and vocabulary.

| Audience | Emphasis | What They Want to Feel |
|---|---|---|
| **Investor** | Market, mechanism, why now | "This could be big and they understand why" |
| **Potential user** | Pain, relief, outcome | "This was built for me" |
| **Technical peer** | Mechanism, architecture insight, interesting problem | "That's a smart way to solve it" |
| **Non-technical stakeholder** | Problem, outcome, simplicity | "I understand what this does" |
| **Accelerator / programme** | Problem, team, traction | "They have conviction and signal" |

Build your primary pitch first. Then identify which audience you will most frequently encounter and tune it for them.

---

## The Structure That Works

This is not the only valid structure. It is the one that fails least often across audiences.

**For an investor or accelerator:**

> You know how [specific user] [specific problem with consequence]?
> We built [product name] — it [mechanism in one sentence].
> Unlike [alternative], we [differentiation].
> We're already [traction signal, even if small].

**For a potential user:**

> If you're a [specific user], you've probably [specific painful moment].
> [Product name] fixes that by [mechanism].
> It takes [time/effort] to set up and you'll [specific outcome] from day one.

**For a technical peer:**

> I'm working on [problem domain]. The interesting thing about it is [mechanism insight].
> Most solutions fail because [failure point]. We sidestep that by [differentiation].
> It's built with [brief stack mention if relevant].

Use the structure that fits your most common audience. Adapt from there.

## Writing Your Pitch

Work through these in order.

### Step 1 — The Hook

The first sentence. It should make the listener think of a person they know — or recognise themselves.

**Weak hook:** "There are 60 million freelancers globally and most of them struggle with invoicing."

**Strong hook:** "You know how freelancers finish a project, open their calendar to write an invoice, and realise they have no idea how many hours they actually worked?"

The strong version describes a specific moment. The listener either knows that moment or can immediately picture someone who does. That recognition is what makes them lean in.

### Step 2 — The Mechanism

One sentence. What the product does and how it does it.

Pull this from your solution statement. Compress it. Remove anything that requires prior context to understand.

"We built a time tracker that logs hours passively by reading your calendar and open apps, so you never have to start or stop a timer" is a mechanism sentence. It tells you what the product does and why it is different in the same breath.

### Step 3 — The Differentiation Signal

One sentence that names what makes your approach distinct without making a vague quality claim.

"Unlike every other time tracker, we remove the habit entirely" is a differentiation signal. "We're simpler and faster than the competition" is not.

### Step 4 — The Traction or Credibility Signal

One sentence. Whatever is most credible about your current position.

In order of strength:
- Revenue ("We have 12 paying customers and $800 MRR")
- Active users with engagement data ("47 users, 80% weekly retention")
- Strong waitlist with conversion ("600 signups, 40% activated in beta")
- Validated demand ("We interviewed 30 freelancers and 28 said they'd pay for this")
- Your credibility ("I spent 5 years as a freelance designer — I lived this problem")

Use whatever you have. If you have nothing yet, lead with your credibility or your validation process. Do not fabricate or inflate.

## Rehearsing Your Pitch

Writing the pitch is 20% of the work. Rehearsal is 80%.

A pitch that lives only on paper will come out stilted, rushed, or over-explained when you actually say it. The goal is to internalise the logic so thoroughly that you can say it naturally — at different speeds, in response to interruptions, in different orders if someone asks a question mid-pitch.

**Rehearsal protocol:**

1. Read the pitch aloud alone, timed. Note where you stumble or slow down — those are the sentences that are not yet natural.

2. Record yourself on your phone. Listen back. You will immediately hear what needs to change.

3. Say it to one person who knows nothing about your product. Watch their face. Confusion is always visible. Ask them to repeat back what they understood — not whether they liked it.

4. Say it to one person who is sceptical. They will ask the hard questions. Your ability to answer without breaking the pitch is the sign it is working.

5. Time it. 60 seconds maximum. If you are going over, you are not cutting enough.

> The pitch is ready when you can say it without thinking about the words — when you are thinking about the listener instead.

## Handling the Questions That Follow

A good pitch always generates questions. These are not interruptions — they are signals that the listener is engaged.

Prepare for the three most common categories:

**Competitive questions:**
> "How is this different from [competitor]?"

Have a specific answer ready. Not "we're simpler" — the actual mechanism difference. If you cannot answer this in one sentence, your differentiation is not yet clear enough.

**Credibility questions:**
> "Why are you the right person to build this?"

Have your WHY YOU from the Idea Definition module ready. One or two sentences. Confident, not defensive.

**Traction questions:**
> "Do you have any customers yet?"

Answer honestly. Small numbers with growth rate beat vague claims. "12 paying customers, up from 3 last month" is better than "we're in early traction."

**Scope questions:**
> "What about [feature or use case]?"

Do not expand the pitch to include everything. "That's on the roadmap — right now we're focused on [core mechanism] for [ICP]." Staying focused signals product discipline.

---

## Validating Your Pitch

**The clarity test:** Three people who know nothing about your product should be able to explain it back to you after hearing it once. If they cannot, the mechanism or the problem is not landing.

**The memory test:** 24 hours after hearing your pitch, a listener should be able to recall the core problem and your mechanism. If they remember only that "it's something to do with invoicing," your mechanism did not stick.

**The traction test:** A good pitch generates one of three responses — a referral, a request for a demo, or a question about pricing. If it consistently generates only polite agreement, it is not creating enough urgency.

## Checklist: Elevator Pitch Complete

**Content**
- [ ] Opens with a specific moment, not a statistic or a category
- [ ] Mechanism is stated in one sentence a non-expert can understand
- [ ] Differentiation is specific — a competitor could not honestly say the same thing
- [ ] Closes with the most credible signal currently available
- [ ] No feature list, no tech stack, no team history in the pitch itself

**Variants**
- [ ] At least two variants exist for different audiences
- [ ] The primary variant is identified and memorised

**Rehearsal**
- [ ] Recorded and listened back to at least once
- [ ] Delivered to at least one person who does not know the product
- [ ] Timed — under 60 seconds at natural speaking pace

**Responses**
- [ ] Competitive question answer prepared
- [ ] Credibility question answer prepared
- [ ] Traction question answered honestly

## What Comes Next

Value Proposition formalises the promise your product makes — the specific, credible claim about what users get and why they should believe it. Where the elevator pitch is what you say, the value proposition is what you write — on your homepage, in your pricing page, in every place a user decides whether to try your product.
