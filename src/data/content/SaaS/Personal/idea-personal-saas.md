# Idea

🕒 **Estimated Time:** 15 Minutes

---

You probably already have an idea.

The question is whether it's the right idea — or a version of the right idea — or a solution looking for a problem.

This module helps you sharpen it into something you can actually build, ship, and validate.

---

## The Idea Is Not the Product

Most people arrive with a solution in mind.

*"I want to build a tool that does X."*

That's fine as a starting point. But a solution is not an idea. An idea is the intersection of three things:

```
Problem worth solving
      ↓
Person who has it (your persona)
      ↓
Solution only you would build this way
```

If any of these are weak, the idea is weak. You can have a real problem and a real persona and still build the wrong solution. You can have a clever solution and no one who actually needs it.

Pressure-test all three before you start building.

## The Idea Sharpening Questions

Work through these in order. Each one narrows the scope.

**1. What is the exact problem?**
Not the category. The specific, observable moment of failure.

Bad: *"People struggle with productivity."*
Good: *"Freelancers forget to follow up on unpaid invoices because they have no system that prompts them at the right time."*

**2. Why does this problem exist today?**
Is it a behavior problem, a workflow gap, a missing tool, or a coordination failure? Understanding the root cause tells you what kind of solution will actually work.

**3. What's the current workaround?**
You identified this in your persona. Now ask: why hasn't the workaround become the solution? What does it not do that your product will?

**4. What's the smallest version of your solution that proves the core idea?**
Not MVP as in "minimum viable product." MVP as in: what is the one thing your product must do that nothing else does? Everything else can come later.

**5. Why you? Why now?**
You don't need a unique insight. But you should have *some* answer to why this is the right problem for you to work on. You know the user, you have the skills, you've felt the pain — something.

## The Idea Traps

These are the patterns that produce projects that never ship.

---

**The "Better Version" Trap**

Building a better Notion, a better Slack, a better Trello.

The problem isn't that incumbents can't be beaten. It's that "better" is not a wedge. Users don't switch tools for marginal improvements. They switch when something does a specific thing for a specific person in a way nothing else does.

*Better for whom? In what specific situation? What do they get that they can't get anywhere else?*

---

**The Complexity Trap**

The idea that requires twelve features to be useful.

If your product only delivers value when everything is built, you can't validate anything early. You'll spend six months building before you know if any of it works.

Ask: *"What is the one-feature version of this idea that still solves the problem?"*

If you can't answer that, the idea needs rethinking — not more features.

---

**The "Everyone" Trap**

*"This could be used by freelancers, small businesses, agencies, students..."*

The wider your target, the weaker every decision becomes. Your onboarding, your copy, your pricing, your core features — all of them get blurred by trying to serve too many people at once.

Your persona already solved this. Hold to it.

---

**The Passion Trap**

Building something you find interesting rather than something a real person needs.

Passion keeps you going when things get hard — and things will get hard. But passion alone doesn't validate demand. The question is not *"do I want to build this?"* It's *"does anyone need this enough to change their behavior for it?"*

## From Idea to Positioning

Once your idea is sharp, you need one sentence that captures it.

This is not a tagline. It's a positioning statement — for your own clarity.

**Format:**

> *"[Product] helps [persona] [do/achieve/avoid] [outcome] — unlike [current alternative] which [specific failure]."*

**Example:**

> *"Flowdesk helps freelance designers track client deliverables and send invoices automatically — unlike Notion which requires manual updates and has no billing logic."*

Write this before you move to Features. If you can't write it cleanly, your idea still has gaps.

## Idea vs. Execution

A common belief: the idea is what matters. Good execution on a bad idea fails. So find a great idea first.

A more accurate belief: **ideas are cheap, but bad ideas waste good execution.**

You don't need a breakthrough insight. You need a problem that's real, a persona that's specific, and a solution that's differentiated enough that someone would choose it over their current workaround.

Most successful solo SaaS products are not novel. They are focused.

They do one thing, for one type of person, better than the general-purpose tools that person is currently duct-taping together.

That's enough.

## Validation Before Building

You don't need to build to validate.

Three lightweight ways to test your idea before writing a line of code:

**1. The conversation test**
Describe your idea in one sentence to someone who matches your persona. Watch their face. If they say "oh I've been wanting something like that" — that's signal. If they say "huh, interesting" — that's not.

**2. The search test**
Search for your idea on Reddit, Indie Hackers, Twitter, and Product Hunt. Are people asking for this? Are competitors already doing it? Both are good signs. Nobody talking about it is a yellow flag.

**3. The landing page test**
Build a one-page site describing the product. Put an email signup on it. Share it where your persona hangs out. If you get signups without a product — you have demand. If you get nothing — you have a data point.

None of these require code. All of them are faster than six months of building.

## Idea Checklist

- [ ] Problem is specific — a moment of failure, not a category
- [ ] Root cause of the problem identified
- [ ] Current workaround named — that's your real competition
- [ ] One-feature version of the solution defined
- [ ] "Why you, why now" answered honestly
- [ ] None of the four traps apply (or you've consciously accepted the risk)
- [ ] Positioning statement written cleanly
- [ ] At least one lightweight validation done before building
