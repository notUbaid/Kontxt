# Pitch Deck

**Estimated Time:** 45–60 min

---

A pitch deck is not a presentation with the speaker removed.

It is a document that must work without you in the room — forwarded to a partner, read at 11pm, opened on a phone. Every slide must carry its own weight because there is no one to explain it.

This module teaches you how experienced founders think about pitch decks at the early stage: what slides matter, what kills decks fast, and how to build one that gets a response even when your product is still young.

---

## Who Is This Deck For?

Before writing a single slide, answer this.

| Audience | What They Are Evaluating | What They Will Do With It |
|---|---|---|
| **Angel investor** | Founder, problem, market, early traction | Forward to partner, schedule a call, or pass |
| **Accelerator (YC, etc.)** | Problem clarity, growth potential, team | Score against hundreds of applications |
| **Professor / academic** | Technical depth, product thinking, execution | Grade or provide feedback |
| **Early customer / partner** | Whether this solves their problem | Decide to trial or refer |
| **Competition judge** | Innovation, viability, presentation quality | Score against a rubric |

The deck you send an angel investor should not look like the one you submit to a class. Same product, different emphasis, different structure, different length.

Decide now. Build for one audience first.

---

## What Kills a Deck Fast

Experienced readers reject decks in under two minutes. Here is what triggers it.

> **Vague problem statement.** "People waste time on manual processes" — this describes every SaaS ever built. If the problem is not specific, the reader cannot evaluate whether it is real.

> **Market size pulled from thin air.** "$50 billion TAM" with no source or methodology signals that the founder has not done the work.

> **Feature list masquerading as a solution slide.** A list of bullet points is not a solution. It is a feature backlog. Show how the product changes the user's life.

> **No traction when traction exists.** Founders hide small numbers. Investors know what early looks like. Hiding 47 signups or 3 paying customers when you have them reads as either dishonesty or lack of awareness.

> **Team slide with no relevant credentials.** "Passionate about the problem" is not a credential. Show why you are the right person to build this, even if your credentials are unconventional.

> **Deck longer than 12 slides.** After slide 12 the reader is already deciding. Every additional slide is a reason to stop.

## The Slide Order That Works

This is not the only valid structure. It is the one that works most reliably at the early stage.

```
1   Problem
2   Solution
3   Product (demo / screenshots)
4   Market
5   Business Model
6   Traction
7   Roadmap
8   Team
9   The Ask
```

Nine slides. Everything else is optional or belongs in an appendix.

The order is deliberate: problem before solution, product before market, traction before team. Readers build conviction linearly. Breaking the order forces them to re-evaluate earlier slides.

## Slide by Slide

### 1 — Problem

The most important slide. If this does not land, nothing else matters.

**What it must do:** Make the reader feel the pain without needing to see the product.

**Structure that works:**
- One headline that names the problem precisely
- One concrete example (a specific person, a specific moment of friction)
- Optional: one data point that quantifies the scale

**What to avoid:**
- Starting with industry statistics that don't connect to a person
- A problem so broad it applies to everyone (and therefore no one)
- Jargon the reader might not recognise

**Test:** Can someone read this slide and immediately name the type of person who has this problem?

---

### 2 — Solution

One sentence. One clear value proposition.

Not how it works. What it does for the user.

**Weak:** "A platform that uses AI to optimise workflows and reduce friction across multiple touchpoints."

**Strong:** "Freelancers log their hours automatically and generate invoices in one click."

If your solution sentence requires explanation, rewrite it. The explanation belongs in the product slide.

---

### 3 — Product

This is where you prove the solution exists.

Use real screenshots, a short screen recording embedded as a GIF, or a clear flow diagram. Do not use mockups if you have a working product. Showing mockups when the product is live signals that you do not trust what you built.

**What to show:**
- The core user action that delivers the value
- The moment the problem disappears

**One to three visuals maximum.** Cramming every screen into this slide makes it unreadable.

If your product is not yet built, be honest. "Here is the interface we are building" with a high-fidelity mockup is defensible. Presenting mockups as if they were a live product is not.

---

### 4 — Market

Investors and judges evaluate market size differently than founders think.

The mistake: starting with TAM (total addressable market) and working down.

The better approach: start with who your first customers are and build up.

```
SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)
"Freelance designers in the US who bill by the hour"
~2.3 million people

↑

TAM (Total Addressable Market)
"All freelancers globally who invoice clients"
~60 million people
```

Show the SAM first. Explain why you can reach them. Then note the TAM to show the ceiling.

**On market size numbers:** Source everything. "According to Statista, 2024" is better than a number with no attribution. If you cannot source it, do not use it.

---

### 5 — Business Model

How do you make money? Be specific.

| What to include | Example |
|---|---|
| Pricing model | $12/month per user, billed annually |
| Who pays | The freelancer, not the client |
| Current status | Free during beta, paid launch in [month] |
| Unit economics (if known) | LTV, CAC, payback period |

If you do not have a business model yet, say so and explain what you are testing. Leaving this slide vague or missing entirely reads as avoidance.

---

### 6 — Traction

This is the slide that separates early-stage decks from idea decks. Show everything real you have.

**Traction is not just revenue.** Early signals count.

```
Strong traction signals
────────────────────────────────────────────
Paying customers (even 1)
Monthly recurring revenue (even $50)
Active users with retention data
Waitlist with strong conversion rate
Letters of intent from potential customers
Pilot agreements
Strong week-over-week growth (even from small base)

Weak but acceptable traction signals
────────────────────────────────────────────
Signups (with conversion rate to active)
User interviews conducted (with quotes)
Search Console impressions for target keywords
Beta users giving consistent positive feedback
```

Do not hide small numbers. 12 paying customers is real traction. Present it with the growth rate: "12 paying customers, up from 3 last month."

**Never fabricate or round up significantly.** The first question after a traction slide is "walk me through these numbers." You need to be able to.

---

### 7 — Roadmap

One slide. Six months maximum of detail.

Show what you are building next and why. Tie it to traction signals if you have them.

**What investors and judges want to see:**
- You have a prioritised view, not just a wish list
- Next milestones are specific and achievable
- You know what "done" looks like for each phase

Refer to your ROADMAP.md from the Roadmap module. Extract the NOW and NEXT items. Do not copy the whole document.

**Format that works:** A simple three-column visual — Now / Next / Later — with two or three items per column.

---

### 8 — Team

At the personal project stage, this is likely a team of one. That is fine.

What matters: why are **you** the right person to build this?

**Relevant credentials include:**
- Domain expertise in the problem space ("I was a freelance designer for 4 years")
- Technical depth that matches the build ("built three production SaaS products")
- Unique access ("I have relationships with 200 freelancers in this community")
- Academic background that is directly relevant

**If you are a student:** Say so. Then immediately explain what you have shipped. Shipped code is more credible than a degree at the early stage.

**If you have advisors or mentors:** Name them here, briefly.

---

### 9 — The Ask

What do you want from the reader?

Be specific. "Looking for feedback" and "open to conversations" are not asks.

| Context | Specific Ask |
|---|---|
| Seeking investment | "$50K to reach 100 paying customers by Q3" |
| Accelerator application | "Acceptance into the next cohort" |
| Class submission | Implied — skip this slide |
| Early customers | "Access to 5 pilot users from your network" |
| Community showcase | "Beta signups — link in the description" |

Include your contact information on this slide. Make it trivial to reply.

## The Appendix

Put everything that matters to some readers but not all readers in an appendix.

Good appendix candidates:
- Full financial model
- Detailed competitive analysis
- Technical architecture diagram
- Extended team bios
- Customer testimonials or case studies
- Detailed unit economics

The appendix signals thoroughness without cluttering the main deck.

## Design Principles

The goal of deck design is to remove friction between the reader and the idea. Not to impress with visual creativity.

**What works:**
- One consistent font family throughout
- Maximum two accent colours
- Enough whitespace that each slide breathes
- Every image and chart at high resolution
- Consistent alignment (use a grid, not visual judgment)

**What doesn't:**
- Dark backgrounds with white text (hard to read in many environments, prints badly)
- Animation and transitions (not visible when forwarded as PDF)
- Stock photos that do not show your actual product
- Logos for technologies you used (no one cares)
- Clip art, gradients from 2015, or templates that look like templates

> **Send as PDF, always.** Never send a Keynote or PowerPoint file. Fonts shift, layouts break, animations disappear. PDF is what the reader sees, exactly.

## AI Prompt: Problem and Solution Slides

Use this to sharpen your two most important slides.

```
I am building a SaaS called [name].

The problem I am solving: [describe it in your own words]
My target user: [specific person]
My solution in one sentence: [your current draft]

Help me:
1. Rewrite the problem statement as a single compelling headline, then a two-sentence explanation using a concrete example of a specific user in a specific moment of pain
2. Identify any vagueness in the problem that would make a reader think "this could describe anything"
3. Rewrite my solution sentence — it should describe what changes for the user, not how the product works
4. Give me two alternative framings of the problem and solution pair, and explain which one is strongest and why

Do not make the language sound like marketing. It should sound like a founder who understands the problem deeply.
```

## AI Prompt: Full Deck Review

Use this after you have a draft of all slides.

```
Review the following pitch deck for a personal SaaS project.

Target audience: [angel investor / accelerator / class / community]
Stage: [pre-revenue / beta / paying customers]

Slide contents:
[paste each slide title and its content]

For each slide:
1. Does it do its job in the narrative? If not, what is missing?
2. Is there anything that would make an experienced reader stop trusting the deck?
3. Is there anything that should be cut or moved to an appendix?

Overall:
4. What is the single weakest slide and how should it be rewritten?
5. Is the problem-solution narrative clear to someone who has never heard of this product?
6. What one question will every reader have after reading this deck that is not answered?

Be direct. Flag anything that sounds like a first-time founder making a common mistake.
```

## Validating AI Output on Decks

AI is useful for editing copy and stress-testing narrative logic. It cannot tell you whether your market is real or your traction is compelling.

**Do not trust AI when it:**
- Validates your market size numbers (it cannot verify them)
- Tells you the deck is "strong" or "compelling" without specific critique
- Writes your problem statement without knowing your users
- Generates traction numbers or unit economics you do not have

**Do trust AI when it:**
- Points out vague language and rewrites it specifically
- Identifies logical gaps in the narrative flow
- Suggests what a reader might not understand
- Rewrites bullet points into cleaner single sentences

The best use of AI on a pitch deck is as a hostile reader who asks "so what?" after every slide.

## The Self-Review Checklist

Read your deck as if you have never heard of the product. Then answer these.

**Problem**
- [ ] Can I name exactly who has this problem?
- [ ] Is there a concrete example of the pain on this slide?
- [ ] Does this feel specific or does it describe every SaaS?

**Solution**
- [ ] Does my solution sentence describe what changes for the user?
- [ ] Is it free of jargon and feature language?

**Product**
- [ ] Am I showing the real product, not a mockup, if the product exists?
- [ ] Is the core value moment visible in the screenshots or recording?

**Market**
- [ ] Are my numbers sourced?
- [ ] Do I show SAM before TAM?

**Business Model**
- [ ] Is there a specific price on this slide?
- [ ] Is the current status (free / beta / paid) clear?

**Traction**
- [ ] Am I showing everything real I have, including small numbers?
- [ ] If I have no traction, am I honest about it rather than hiding the slide?

**Roadmap**
- [ ] Is this tied to real signals, not a wish list?
- [ ] Is it six months of detail maximum?

**Team**
- [ ] Have I explained why I am the right person to build this?

**The Ask**
- [ ] Is there a specific number or outcome on this slide?
- [ ] Is my contact information present?

**Overall**
- [ ] Is the deck under 12 slides (excluding appendix)?
- [ ] Is it exported as PDF?
- [ ] Has someone unfamiliar with the product read it and understood it?
