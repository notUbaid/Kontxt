# Pitch Deck

Your presentation story is set. Now it becomes slides — and for an internal tool specifically, the deck's job is to make an unglamorous but real problem (a messy spreadsheet, a manual approval process) visually obvious as a problem worth solving, before you ever show the fix.

## The Decision You're Actually Making

Not "what slides do we need." It's: **can a judge glance at slide 2 and immediately recognize the pain, even if they've never done this exact job themselves?**

Internal tool problems are often mundane by nature — that's not a weakness, it's what makes them relatable. The deck's job is to make that mundane pain visible and specific, not to dress it up as something more dramatic than it is.

## A Slide Structure Built for This Category

| Slide | Purpose | Internal-tool-specific note |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Title + one-line pitch | Sets context | Name the specific process, not a vague category ("PTO tracking," not "HR tool") |
| 2. The current pain | Makes the problem visceral | Show the actual messy spreadsheet/process if you can, even a screenshot |
| 3. The fix, live cue | What you built | Cue for your core workflow action, not a feature list |
| 4. The bulk operation moment | Your strongest visual proof | A before/after: "10 clicks" vs "1 click" |
| 5. What's next | Shows deliberate scoping | One sentence from your deferred list |

> ** Best Practice:** If you have a real or realistic screenshot of the "before" state — a cluttered spreadsheet, a long email thread — put it directly on slide 2. Seeing the actual mess is far more persuasive than a bullet point describing it, and it costs nothing extra to include if you already have (or can quickly mock) that reference.

## Making the Bulk Operation Slide Concrete

This is often your best visual moment — make the slide reflect that, not just a generic feature callout.

```
Weak: "Includes bulk operations feature"

Strong: A before/after visual — "Before: 10 individual clicks to 
approve 10 requests" next to "After: Select all, click once"
```

> ** Tip:** A simple before/after count (10 clicks → 1 click) is more convincing on a slide than an adjective like "faster" or "more efficient" — it's a specific, verifiable claim that a judge can immediately understand and remember after you've moved to the next project.

## What to Leave Off

- Database schema or architecture diagrams — save for questions, not the main deck
- A comprehensive feature list — pick the 2-3 that matter, per your Presentation Prep work
- Generic productivity language ("streamline," "optimize") without the specific number or example behind it

> **️ Warning:** Avoid vague efficiency claims without a concrete backing example. "Streamlines your workflow" says nothing memorable; "turns a 10-step spreadsheet process into 2 clicks" is specific, credible, and exactly what a judge remembers when comparing your project to the next one they see.

## Generate the Deck Outline with AI

**Prompt — Internal Tool Pitch Deck Outline**
```
I built an internal tool for [specific process] that replaces 
[specific current manual method]. Here's what I built:
[core fix, any bulk operation with before/after numbers, role 
awareness if relevant]

Draft a 5-slide outline: title, current pain (with a visual cue if I 
have a "before" screenshot), the fix live cue, the bulk operation 
before/after, and what's next. For each slide, write the on-screen 
text (max 15 words) and a one-sentence speaker note.
```

> ** Token Efficiency:** Reuse the same conversation from Presentation Prep — the pain description and demo sequencing you already worked out there carries directly into deck content without re-explaining the project.

## Validate Before You Present

- Slide 2 makes the current pain visually or specifically concrete, not just described in words
- The bulk operation (if built) is shown as a specific before/after, not a vague feature mention
- No slide relies on generic efficiency language without a concrete number or example behind it
- Architecture/schema details are absent from the main deck

## Common Mistakes

- Describing the pain abstractly instead of showing or specifically naming the current messy process
- Vague "makes things more efficient" language without a specific, memorable number
- Feature-listing everything built instead of highlighting the 2-3 most convincing pieces
- Including architecture diagrams that belong in Q&A, not the core pitch

## Quick Reference

| Must-have | Nice-to-have | Skip |
|---|---|---|
| Specific current-pain slide | Real "before" screenshot | Architecture/schema diagrams |
| Concrete before/after numbers | Role-awareness visual | Comprehensive feature list |
| One highlighted core fix | Deferred feature callout | Generic efficiency language |
