# Roadmap

You've shipped, you've collected feedback, and you've found real patterns in what people actually experience using your app. This module is about turning that into a plan — but a plan that fits what a personal project actually is: something you're building because you want to, on your own time, without the pressure of a team or a business depending on the outcome.

The goal here isn't a formal product roadmap. It's an honest answer to "what do I actually want to do with this next?"

---

## The Core Idea: A Personal Roadmap's Real Constraint Is Your Own Motivation, Not Resources

A company roadmap is constrained by budget, headcount, and deadlines. Yours is constrained by something different and easy to forget: your own continued interest in the project. The most common way personal projects die isn't a technical wall — it's the builder losing motivation because the next steps felt like obligations instead of things they actually wanted to do.

> ** Tip**
> Before planning what to build next, ask yourself honestly: do I still want to work on this? If the answer is genuinely no, that's useful information too — not every personal project needs to become a permanent commitment, and consciously deciding to wind one down is different from quietly abandoning it out of guilt.

---

## Step 1: Sort What You've Learned Into Three Honest Buckets

Using the patterns surfaced in the Feedback module, sort what you know into categories that reflect what a personal project actually needs — not a business prioritization framework borrowed from a company roadmap.

**Decision Card — Personal Roadmap Triage**

| Bucket | What Goes Here | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Fixes I'd want even if no one else used this | Real bugs, real friction in the core use case you built this for | A crash, a confusing flow you also find annoying yourself |
| Things multiple people independently mentioned | Patterns from feedback, not one-off comments | Several testers got stuck at the same step |
| Things that sound fun to build, regardless of feedback | Features you're genuinely excited about, even with no external pressure to build them | You just want to try implementing something interesting |

Unlike a company roadmap, the third bucket is legitimate here — and sometimes the most important one. A personal project is allowed to prioritize what's enjoyable to build, not only what's measurably justified.

> ** Warning**
> Don't let an unprioritized feedback backlog quietly turn into a list of obligations. If every item on your list feels like something you "should" do rather than something you want to do, you've built a job for yourself out of a side project — and that's a common reason people stop working on things they otherwise enjoyed.

---

## Step 2: Decide on a Realistic Pace, Not an Aspirational One

Personal projects don't need sprints or deadlines, but they do benefit from being honest about how much time you'll actually have. A roadmap sized for "if I had unlimited weekends" will just sit there making you feel behind.

**Best Practice Card — Realistic Personal Pacing**

```
Instead of: "I'll ship offline support, push notifications, and a
            redesign this month"

Try: "I have maybe 3-4 hours most weekends. I'll pick the single
     highest-value item from my honest buckets and work on just
     that until it's done, then revisit the list."

One thing actually finished beats five things half-started. A
personal project roadmap with too many simultaneous threads is a
common way motivation quietly drains away.
```

---

## Step 3: Decide What You're Explicitly Not Doing — And Let That Be Okay

Just like the production and hackathon curricula, an honest "not now" (or even "not ever") list protects your time and your motivation. For a personal project specifically, it's also fine to decide something is permanently out of scope simply because you don't want to build it — no business justification required.

> ** Note**
> "I don't want to build that" is a complete answer for a personal project, even if a piece of feedback suggested it and even if it would objectively make the app better. You're not obligated to build everything that would help — you're building something you want to keep working on.

## Using AI to Plan Without Losing the Personal Part

AI is useful here for the same organizational tasks as in larger projects — but the prompt needs to explicitly protect your actual motivation and pace, since a generic "build a roadmap" prompt will default to business-style prioritization frameworks that don't fit a personal project's real constraints.

**Prompt: A Roadmap Sized to Real Personal Time**

```
This is a personal mobile app project, not a business. Here's what
I've learned from feedback so far:

[paste your triaged buckets from Step 1, or raw feedback themes]

I have roughly [X hours per week/weekend] to work on this, and I want
to stay motivated, not turn this into an obligation.

1. Suggest a realistic next 2-3 items to focus on, prioritizing real
   bugs and multi-person feedback patterns first, but also flag if
   any feedback-driven item would genuinely conflict with what
   sounds like fun, sustainable work for a side project.
2. Suggest what to explicitly defer or drop, and why.
3. Don't suggest a roadmap sized for more time than I actually have.
```

> ** Why this prompt works**
> Explicitly stating the personal, non-business framing and the real time constraint prevents the model from defaulting to a denser, business-style roadmap structure that would set you up to feel behind almost immediately. Asking it to flag potential conflicts between feedback-driven items and "fun, sustainable work" surfaces the real risk named in Step 1 — turning your own project into an obligation — rather than treating every piece of feedback as an automatic priority.

**Token efficiency note:** Revisit this kind of planning conversation only when your available time or motivation genuinely shifts — there's no cadence requirement here the way there might be for a team roadmap. A personal project roadmap can be revisited whenever it actually feels useful to you, not on a forced schedule.

---

## Validating the Roadmap Feels Right

- [ ] You can honestly say you're still interested in working on this — if not, that's worth sitting with before committing to a list
- [ ] The plan is sized to real available time, not an aspirational amount
- [ ] At least one item on the list is there because it sounds genuinely enjoyable to build, not only because feedback demanded it
- [ ] You have an explicit, guilt-free "not doing this" list
- [ ] The plan focuses on finishing one thing at a time rather than spreading effort across several half-started threads
