# Demo Script

The best app in the room loses to the best-presented app in the room.

Every time.

A demo script is not about memorising lines. It is about knowing exactly what you will show, in what order, and what you will say while showing it — so that when nerves hit and time pressure mounts, you execute cleanly.

Improvised demos have improvised problems.

---

## The Structure of a Winning Demo

```
Opening      30 sec   — Who you are, what the app does, why it matters
Problem      30 sec   — Make the pain real and specific
Live Demo   90 sec   — Walk the core flow, narrate the value
Tech moment  30 sec   — One impressive technical detail
Close        30 sec   — Vision + ask
```

Total: ~3.5 minutes. Leave 90 seconds for questions if your slot is 5 minutes.

Every second is allocated before you walk up.

---

## Write Your Script

Do not wing the opening. The first 30 seconds set the tone for everything that follows.

Write it word for word. Practise it until it sounds natural.

## The Demo Flow

Map your live demo to specific taps before you present. Not "show the home screen" — the exact sequence.

**Template — fill this in for your app:**

```
[00:00] Open app — already on Home screen, signed in as demo account
[00:05] Narrate: "This is [App Name]. The first thing a user sees is..."
[00:15] Tap into [core feature screen]
[00:20] Narrate: "Here's where the magic happens — [what it does]"
[00:35] Perform the core action: [tap / input / capture / generate]
[00:45] Show the result
[00:50] Narrate: "And this is what they get — [value delivered]"
[01:05] Navigate to [secondary feature]
[01:15] Narrate: "We also built [secondary feature] because [reason]"
[01:30] Show native feature (camera / location / haptic / etc.)
[01:45] Narrate: "On mobile, we can also [native capability]"
```

## The Narration Principles

What you say while tapping matters as much as what you show.

**Narrate the value, not the action.**

Weak: "So here I'm tapping the button and it loads the screen."
Strong: "One tap — and in under two seconds, they have their answer."

**Name the user, not yourself.**

Weak: "I can now see my feed."
Strong: "Sarah opens the app and immediately sees everything she cares about."

**Anticipate the next beat.**

Before each tap: "Now watch what happens when..."
After each result: "That's [the value] — instantly."

**Silence is fine.** Let impressive moments breathe for 2–3 seconds before narrating.

## Handling the Live Demo Device

The demo device is not your development device.

**Before you walk up:**
- Screen brightness: maximum
- Do Not Disturb: on
- Notifications: silenced
- Battery: above 80%
- App: open, on the correct starting screen
- Demo account: signed in
- Network: confirmed working (or demo data cached for offline)
- Screen mirroring: tested and confirmed

**During the demo:**
- Hold the phone naturally — portrait, camera facing audience if mirroring
- Tap deliberately — not fast, not nervously
- If something goes wrong: acknowledge it once, move on. "Let me show you this on the next screen instead." Never freeze.

## The Backup Plan

Something will go wrong. Plan for it before it happens.

| Failure | Recovery |
|---|---|
| App crashes | Have a screen recording ready as a fallback slide |
| Network unavailable | Ensure demo data works offline; test this explicitly |
| Feature doesn't load | "Here's what that looks like when it works" → show mockup screenshot |
| Ran out of time | Know which section to cut — practise the short version |
| Lost train of thought | Return to your opening line: "What we built is [one sentence]" |

**Non-negotiable:** have a 60-second screen recording of your core flow exported and ready in your pitch deck as a fallback video slide.

## The Technical Moment

Every technical hackathon expects at least one moment where you demonstrate that what you built is genuinely hard.

Pick one of:
- The AI call in real time ("I'm going to type a prompt and show you what the model returns in under 3 seconds")
- The native feature in action ("Watch — the camera detects this in real time")
- The realtime sync ("I'll open it on two devices simultaneously")
- The edge case you handled ("Most apps break here. Watch what ours does.")

One moment. Make it land.

## Q&A Preparation

Judges ask variations of six questions. Prepare answers for all of them.

| Question | What They're Really Asking |
|---|---|
| "How does the AI work?" | Is this a wrapper or something real? |
| "How would you monetise this?" | Have you thought beyond the hackathon? |
| "What's the main technical challenge you solved?" | Did you learn anything? |
| "Why mobile and not web?" | Is native justified? |
| "What would you build next?" | Is there a roadmap or is this a dead end? |
| "Who else is doing this?" | Do you know your competitive landscape? |

## Rehearsal Protocol

**Solo run:** Say the full demo out loud, on the device, timed. Do this at least twice.

**Team run:** Full demo in front of at least one teammate who has not seen it. They play the judge. Ask them: what was unclear? What was most impressive?

**Timing check:** If you ran over — cut. If you finished early — don't add, tighten.

**The night before:** Run it once on the demo device in the actual demo environment if possible. Network conditions, lighting, and screen mirroring behave differently in different rooms.

## Demo Script Checklist

- [ ] Written opening — first 30 seconds scripted word for word
- [ ] Beat-by-beat demo flow mapped with timestamps
- [ ] Narration focuses on user value, not app mechanics
- [ ] One clear technical moment identified and practised
- [ ] Backup screen recording exported and in pitch deck
- [ ] Demo device configured (DND, brightness, battery, signed in)
- [ ] Q&A answers prepared for all six common questions
- [ ] Solo run completed and timed
- [ ] Team run completed with feedback incorporated
- [ ] Short version (60 sec) practised in case of time cuts

## The Mindset

You built something real in 48 hours or less.

That is already impressive.

The demo script exists not because you need to convince anyone you're capable — it exists because judges have limited attention, limited time, and see too many things. Your script is a gift to them: a clear, efficient path to understanding what you made and why it matters.

Respect their time. Know your demo cold. Let the product do the rest.
