# Presentation Prep

You are done building.

Everything from here is about how clearly you can communicate what you built, why it matters, and why your team is the one to build it.

Presentation prep is not rehearsing lines. It is removing every variable that could go wrong when you are standing in front of judges.

---

## The Night Before

These are not optional. Do all of them.

**Device:**
- [ ] Demo device fully charged
- [ ] Do Not Disturb enabled — and tested (some devices have "breakthrough" settings that still let calls through)
- [ ] Screen brightness at maximum
- [ ] Screen timeout set to never (or maximum) during the demo
- [ ] Screen mirroring tested with the exact cable or adapter you're bringing
- [ ] App open on the correct starting screen

**App:**
- [ ] Full demo flow run once on demo device, in demo conditions
- [ ] Demo account signed in
- [ ] Demo data confirmed loaded
- [ ] Backup screen recording confirmed playable from pitch deck

**Pitch deck:**
- [ ] Exported as PDF (PowerPoint and Keynote files corrupt on other machines)
- [ ] Uploaded to Google Drive, Dropbox, or iCloud — accessible without your laptop
- [ ] Opened and scrolled through on the presentation machine if possible
- [ ] Fonts embedded or substitution checked

**Team:**
- [ ] Everyone knows their role: who speaks, who demos, who handles Q&A
- [ ] Handoffs between speakers practised (nothing kills momentum like an awkward "uh, take it away")
- [ ] One person owns the device. Only one.

---

## The Morning Of

**90 minutes before:**
- Eat. Seriously.
- Final solo run of the demo, out loud
- Confirm your slot time and location

**30 minutes before:**
- Arrive at the venue or room
- Test screen mirroring on the actual display in the room
- Confirm WiFi or switch to mobile hotspot if the venue network is unreliable
- Open the app, navigate to start screen, lock it there

**5 minutes before:**
- Device in hand, screen on, app open
- Deck on the first slide
- Team in position
- One deep breath

## Owning the Room

You have the judges' attention for 3–5 minutes. How you carry yourself in that time matters as much as what you say.

**Stand to the side of the screen**, not in front of it. Judges need to see your product, not your back.

**Make eye contact with judges**, not with your device or deck. Hold it for 2–3 seconds per person. It signals confidence.

**Slow down.** The urge under pressure is to rush. Slow is clear. Clear is convincing.

**When you show something impressive, pause.** Let judges see it. Silence for 2 seconds after a strong moment lands harder than immediately narrating over it.

**If something breaks:** one sentence, move on. "We had an issue with that — let me show you what that looks like here." Then show the backup. Do not apologise repeatedly. Do not freeze.

---

## The Handoff Protocol

If multiple people are presenting, transitions are where demos fall apart.

**Weak handoff:** "Uh, yeah so, I'll pass it to Priya now."

**Strong handoff:** Complete your thought, end on a cue word your teammate knows, make eye contact with them. They begin immediately.

Practise this explicitly. Run through every handoff at least three times.

---

## Q&A Posture

When a judge asks a question:

1. **Pause before answering.** One second of thought signals confidence, not uncertainty.
2. **Answer the question that was asked.** Not the question you wish they asked.
3. **Be honest about limitations.** "We didn't get to that yet, but here's how we'd approach it" beats a vague non-answer every time.
4. **If you don't know:** "That's something we'd want to validate — our current assumption is X." Never make up an answer to a technical question.

The best Q&A answer is short, direct, and specific.

## What Judges Actually Score

Different hackathons weight differently, but most judge on some version of:

| Criterion | What They're Looking For |
|---|---|
| **Innovation** | Is this a new idea, or a new approach to an existing problem? |
| **Technical execution** | Did you actually build this, and is it working? |
| **Design / UX** | Does it feel like a product, not a prototype? |
| **Impact / problem fit** | Does the problem matter? Does this solve it? |
| **Presentation** | Can they communicate clearly under pressure? |
| **Completeness** | Is there a full flow, or just one working screen? |

Know which criteria your specific hackathon weights most. Read the judging rubric if one is published. Orient your pitch accordingly.

## The Mindset Going In

You will not have built everything you wanted to build.

Nobody does.

What you ship in a hackathon is never the full vision. It is proof of concept, proof of taste, and proof of execution speed. Judges know this.

Your job is not to apologise for what is missing. Your job is to make the case for what exists and where it goes.

Present what you have with full conviction. The team that believes in their own product is the team that wins.

## After the Presentation

Whatever the result: debrief as a team.

Ask each other:
- What landed?
- What confused people?
- What would we cut next time?
- What would we lead with?

The habit of honest retrospective is the thing that compounds across hackathons. Most teams who win their second hackathon lost their first.
