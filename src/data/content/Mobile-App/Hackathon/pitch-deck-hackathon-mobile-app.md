# Pitch Deck

Your pitch deck is not a summary of what you built.

It is an argument for why what you built matters.

Judges review dozens of submissions. They remember stories, not feature lists. A deck that opens with a problem and closes with a vision is remembered. A deck that opens with a tech stack is not.

You have 3–5 minutes. Every slide must earn its place.

---

## The Architecture of a Winning Deck

This is not a rigid template. It is a proven narrative arc.

```
1. Hook          — The moment that makes judges lean in
2. Problem       — The pain, made visceral
3. Solution      — Your app, in one sentence
4. Product       — Show it, don't describe it
5. How It Works  — The mechanic, simply explained
6. Tech / AI     — What makes it possible (brief)
7. Traction      — Any signal that it's real
8. Team          — Why you are the ones to build this
9. Vision        — Where this goes
10. Ask / CTA    — What you want from this moment
```

Not every hackathon needs all ten. Cut ruthlessly. A tight 7-slide deck beats a bloated 14-slide deck every time.

---

## Slide by Slide

### Slide 1 — Hook

One sentence. One image. Maximum impact.

Not: "We built an AI-powered mobile app for X."
Yes: "Every day, 400 million people struggle with X. We built the solution."

Or open with a statistic that makes the problem undeniable. Or a question. Or a provocative statement.

The hook buys you the next 3 minutes of attention. Spend time on it.

---

### Slide 2 — Problem

Make the pain specific and human.

Weak: "There is no good solution for X."
Strong: "When [specific person] tries to [do thing], they [face specific consequence]. This happens [frequency]."

Use one of:
- A real person's story (even a composite)
- A statistic with a source
- A moment judges will recognise from their own life

One problem. One slide. Do not list five sub-problems.

---

### Slide 3 — Solution

Your app name. Your tagline. One sentence that says what it does and for whom.

```
[App Name]
[Tagline]

[One sentence: what it is, who it's for, what it does]
```

This is the reveal. Make it feel like one.

---

### Slide 4 — Product

Your best mockup. Full slide. No bullet points.

Use the Feature Graphic or a hero device mockup from your Play Store assets. Let the product speak.

If you have a 15-second screen recording that shows the core flow — embed it here. A live product beats a static slide.

---

### Slide 5 — How It Works

3 steps. Maximum.

```
Step 1 → Step 2 → Step 3
[icon]     [icon]     [icon]
[label]    [label]    [label]
```

Show the user journey from zero to value. Keep it simple enough that a non-technical judge understands it in 10 seconds.

---

### Slide 6 — Tech and AI Stack

Judges at technical hackathons want to see this. Judges at product hackathons care less.

Read the room. If included:
- Name the key technologies (React Native, Firebase, Anthropic API, etc.)
- Explain what each enables — not just the name
- If you used AI in an interesting way, highlight it here
- One slide, maximum 6 items

```
Copy Prompt ↓
```

> I built a mobile app with this stack: [list your stack]. Write a 6-item tech slide where each item has: the technology name, and one sentence on what it specifically enables in our product. Make it sound technically credible but accessible to a non-engineer. Avoid generic descriptions.

---

### Slide 7 — Traction

Any signal that this is real beyond the hackathon.

| Signal | Example |
|---|---|
| User interviews | "Spoke with 12 people in our target audience — 9 said they'd use this weekly" |
| Beta signups | "47 signups from a landing page we posted 24 hours ago" |
| Market size | "$4.2B TAM, growing 18% YoY" |
| Partnerships | "In conversations with [organisation] about piloting" |
| Personal experience | "We built this because we live this problem" |

No traction signal is too small for a hackathon. No traction signal is strong enough to fake. Use what's real.

---

### Slide 8 — Team

Photo, name, and one credential per person.

The credential should answer: why is this person credible for this problem?

Not: "Full-stack developer, 3 years experience."
Yes: "Former healthcare worker who experienced this problem firsthand." or "Previously built X with Y users."

If your team has relevant domain expertise, this is where you surface it.

---

### Slide 9 — Vision

Where does this go in 2 years if it works?

This is not a roadmap. This is an aspiration.

One sentence. Make judges feel the potential.

"If we get this right, [specific group of people] will never have to [problem] again."

---

### Slide 10 — Close

What do you want right now?

- A vote
- Feedback on a specific decision
- An introduction to someone
- Recognition of a specific achievement

End with your app name, tagline, and a QR code to download or try the app.

## Visual Design Principles

A great deck and a bad deck often contain the same information. The difference is execution.

**One idea per slide.** If you need to split content across two slides — split it.

**Dark backgrounds for mobile apps.** If your app is dark-themed, your deck should be too. Consistency signals craft.

**Your brand colors throughout.** The deck should feel like it was made by the same team that made the app.

**Large text.** Minimum 24pt for body copy. Judges may be viewing from across a table.

**Real screenshots over illustrations.** Show your actual product wherever possible.

```
Copy Prompt ↓
```

> I'm designing a pitch deck for a hackathon mobile app called [App Name]. Brand colors: [primary, secondary, background]. Design style: [minimal / bold / dark / clean]. Generate a slide-by-slide design brief covering: background color, headline font treatment, body text style, accent usage, and one signature visual element that should appear consistently across all slides. Write it as a brief I can hand to a designer or follow in Figma / Canva.

> I'm building a pitch deck for a hackathon mobile app called [App Name]. Here is the context:
>
> Problem: [describe the problem in 2–3 sentences]
> Solution: [describe your app in 2–3 sentences]
> Target user: [who it's for]
> Core features: [list 3–5]
> Tech stack: [list]
> Team: [names and one credential each]
>
> Write slide content for a 7-slide deck following this structure: Hook, Problem, Solution, Product (describe the visual only), How It Works (3 steps), Tech Stack (6 items with explanations), Vision. Each slide: headline + 2–4 lines of body copy maximum. Tone: [confident / conversational / bold]. Write for a technical hackathon audience.

## The Anti-Patterns

| Pattern | Why It Loses |
|---|---|
| Opening with the team slide | Nobody cares yet who you are |
| Feature list instead of story | Lists don't create conviction |
| Technical architecture diagram on slide 2 | Non-technical judges disengage immediately |
| Fonts smaller than 18pt | Unreadable from any distance |
| More than 12 slides for a 5-minute pitch | You will rush and lose coherence |
| "Our app uses AI to..." without explaining how | Sounds like every other submission |
| No product visuals | Judges have no reference point |
| Vague vision ("change the world") | Means nothing without specifics |

---

## Deck Checklist

- [ ] Hook slide grabs attention without explanation
- [ ] Problem is specific and human, not generic
- [ ] Solution slide names the app and states value in one sentence
- [ ] Product slide shows real mockup or screen recording
- [ ] How It Works explained in 3 steps or fewer
- [ ] Tech slide explains what each technology enables, not just names it
- [ ] At least one traction signal included
- [ ] Team slide has photo + relevant credential per person
- [ ] Vision is specific and aspirational
- [ ] Close slide has QR code or app link
- [ ] Deck is 7–10 slides maximum
- [ ] Brand colors and font consistent throughout
- [ ] All text readable at 24pt minimum
- [ ] Play Store mockups used as product visuals
