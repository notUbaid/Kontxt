# You Are Here to Win

Hackathons are not about building the best software.

They are about building the most convincing case — in the time you have — that your idea deserves to exist.

The teams that win are not always the best engineers. They are the teams that made the clearest decisions, built the right things, skipped the right things, and presented with confidence.

Kontxt is going to help you do exactly that.

## The Hackathon Mindset

There are two ways to fail a hackathon:

**Build too much.** You spend 18 hours on a feature nobody will see during the demo. The core flow is broken. You present a half-finished product and hope judges imagine the rest.

**Build too little.** You have a working app with one screen, no data, and no story. Judges see a prototype with no soul.

The winning zone is narrow: **a tight, polished, demo-ready core experience** with a compelling story around it.

Every decision Kontxt helps you make is optimised for that outcome.

## Hackathon Rules of Thumb

These apply regardless of your idea, stack, or team size:

**Judges remember feelings, not features.**
A demo that makes a judge feel something — surprise, delight, "I wish this existed" — beats a demo that lists capabilities.

**Polish beats completeness.**
A beautiful two-screen app beats a functional ten-screen app that looks unfinished. Scope aggressively.

**The demo is the product.**
You are not shipping to users. You are shipping to a 3-minute demo slot. Design every decision around what will be visible and impressive in that window.

**Fake it where it does not matter.**
Hardcoded demo data, mocked API responses, and pre-seeded accounts are not cheating. They are smart. Use them freely for anything outside your core flow.

**Ship at hour 18, polish at hour 22.**
Never be in active development during your last two hours. That time is for fixing, polishing, and rehearsing. Teams that code until submission usually submit broken demos.

## Your Team's First 30 Minutes

Before you open any code editor, do this together:

**1. Say the idea out loud in one sentence.**
If you cannot say it in one sentence, you do not understand it well enough to build it yet. Keep refining until you can.

**2. Describe the demo.**
Walk through the demo as if you are presenting right now. What does the judge see first? What do they interact with? What is the "wow" moment? If you cannot describe a clear wow moment, the idea needs work.

**3. Identify the riskiest assumption.**
What must be true for this idea to work? Name it explicitly. If that assumption is wrong, what is your backup?

**4. Assign roles.**
Who owns design? Who owns backend? Who owns the demo script? Ambiguous ownership kills momentum in hackathons.

**5. Set your scope checkpoint.**
Pick a time — roughly halfway through your build window — where you will evaluate scope and cut anything that is not essential to the demo.
