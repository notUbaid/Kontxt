# Pitch Deck

Your presentation story is set. Now it becomes slides — the visual backbone that makes an abstract concept like "liquidity" or "cold start" concrete enough for someone to grasp at a glance, whether you're sharing this with friends, a community, or just documenting the project properly for yourself.

## The Decision You're Actually Making

Not "what slides do we need." It's: **can someone who's never seen this project understand, from the slides alone, why building a marketplace was hard and what you actually solved?**

Marketplace concepts (liquidity, two-sided trust, cold start) are abstract. Slides need to make them visual and concrete, not just describe them in more bullet points.

## A Marketplace-Specific Slide Structure

| Slide | Purpose | What makes it marketplace-specific |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Title + one-line pitch | Sets context | Name both sides explicitly: "connecting [buyers] with [sellers]" |
| 2. The coordination problem | Why this is hard | Show it as two disconnected sides, not a single user problem |
| 3. Cold-start strategy | How you solved chicken-and-egg | A simple before/after: empty marketplace → first real match |
| 4. Liquidity proof | Real evidence it works | A number or simple chart — sell-through rate, time-to-match |
| 5. Trust mechanism | One thing you're proud of | Reviews, verification, or dispute handling — pick one |
| 6. What's next | Shows ongoing direction | One sentence, informed by real roadmap decisions |

> ** Best Practice:** On the coordination problem slide, visually show two separate groups (buyers, sellers) with a gap between them, then show them connected on the solution slide. This simple before/after visual communicates the actual hard problem — matching two sides — far better than a paragraph explaining it.

## Making Liquidity Visual, Not Just Stated

"Our marketplace has good liquidity" means nothing to someone unfamiliar with the term. Show it instead.

> ** Tip:** A simple bar showing sell-through rate, or a short timeline showing "first listing → first sale" duration, makes liquidity tangible in a way a sentence can't. If your numbers are still small, that's fine — a real, small number is more credible than a vague claim of success.

## What to Leave Off

- Full technical architecture diagrams — save for questions, not the main deck
- Every feature you built — pick the one trust/safety mechanism that's most interesting
- Inflated or projected numbers presented as if they're already proven

> **️ Warning:** Don't present projected future liquidity as if it's current reality. If your marketplace is early-stage, a slide claiming strong liquidity that doesn't yet exist is the fastest way to lose credibility the moment someone asks a specific follow-up question.

## Generate the Deck Outline with AI

**Prompt — Marketplace Pitch Deck Outline**
```
I built a personal-project marketplace connecting [buyers] with 
[sellers] for [describe category]. Here's my real data:
[cold-start approach, current sell-through rate or liquidity signal, 
one trust/safety feature]

Draft a 6-slide outline using this structure: title, coordination 
problem, cold-start strategy, liquidity proof, trust mechanism, 
what's next. For each slide, write the on-screen text (max 15 words) 
and a one-sentence speaker note.
```

> ** Token Efficiency:** Reuse the same conversation from Presentation Prep — the story structure and data you already provided there carries directly into deck content, and re-explaining the project from scratch wastes tokens on context you've already established.

## Validate Before You Present

- The coordination problem slide visually shows two disconnected sides, not just text
- Liquidity is shown as a real number or simple visual, not just claimed in words
- Only one trust/safety mechanism is featured, chosen deliberately, not all of them listed
- No slide presents unproven or projected numbers as current fact

## Common Mistakes

- Describing the marketplace as "an app with listings and payments" instead of a two-sided trust problem solved
- No visual representation of liquidity — only vague claims like "it's growing"
- Listing every feature instead of highlighting the one meaningful trust decision
- Presenting future goals as if they were already achieved

## Quick Reference

| Must-have | Nice-to-have | Skip |
|---|---|---|
| Two-sided coordination visual | Simple liquidity chart | Full architecture diagram |
| Real (even small) liquidity number | Before/after cold-start visual | Every feature listed |
| One highlighted trust mechanism | Category-specific proof point | Inflated/projected metrics |
