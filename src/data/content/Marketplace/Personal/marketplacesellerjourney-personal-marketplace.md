# Seller Journey

## Why This Is Not "Buyer Journey, Mirrored"

It's tempting to treat this as a find-and-replace exercise on the previous module — swap "buyer" for "seller" and move on. Don't. The seller's psychology is fundamentally different.

A buyer is risking money once, for one item. A seller is risking *time, reputation, and recurring effort* — and they're betting that effort on a platform with no proven audience yet. The question every seller asks isn't "can I trust this listing," it's "will this be worth my time."

In a personal-mode marketplace, you have no supply yet. This module exists to make sure the first sellers you recruit by hand don't churn after one bad experience — because at this scale, you can't afford to lose any of them.

---

## The Three Things Every Seller Is Actually Evaluating

| What They Ask | What It Really Means | What Kills It |
|---|---|---|
| "Will anyone actually buy this here?" | Is there demand, or am I shouting into a void? | Empty marketplace, zero buyer activity signal |
| "How much effort does listing take?" | Is the time cost worth the expected payoff? | Long forms, unclear requirements, no save-and-resume |
| "Will I actually get paid, reliably?" | Is the payout mechanism trustworthy and fast? | Vague payout terms, no visibility into transaction status |

> ️ **The cold-start trap:** Your first sellers are joining a marketplace with no buyers yet. They can't see proof it works because the proof doesn't exist. Your job in Phase 1 isn't to convince them the marketplace is thriving — it's to make the cost of trying it low enough that "no buyers yet" isn't a dealbreaker.

## Map Your Specific Seller Journey

Same format as the buyer exercise — one sentence per answer, this is a decision record.

- **Who is the seller, specifically?** (Hobbyist vs. professional changes everything about tolerance for friction)
- **What are they currently doing instead** to sell/offer this? (Another platform, word of mouth, nothing)
- **What's the real cost of listing** — time, photos, information they need to gather?
- **What would make them list once and never come back?**
- **What would make them list and actively recommend the platform to peers?**
- **How do they expect to get paid**, and how fast?

## Reduce Listing Friction Before You Reduce Listing Steps

Founders default to "fewer form fields = less friction." That's only half true. Missing the *right* fields creates more friction later — buyers ask questions in messages that should've been in the listing, sellers get annoyed, listings look thin and untrustworthy.

| Field Type | Personal-Mode Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Core listing info (title, price, description) | Required, minimal — 3-4 fields max |
| Photos | Required, but accept phone photos — don't gate on quality |
| Category/tags | Pick from a short list you define manually, not freeform | 
| Availability/condition specifics | Optional at launch — add only if buyers keep asking in messages |
| Seller verification | Skip for v1 — see Trust & Safety in the next module |

>  **Best practice:** Watch what questions buyers actually ask sellers in your first 10 manually-brokered transactions (from the Buyer Journey validation step). Those questions tell you exactly which optional fields to add — not guesses, not competitor research.

## The Cold-Start Offer: What You Give Early Sellers

Since you can't yet offer "lots of buyers," offer something you *can* deliver at zero scale:

| Offer | Cost to You | Why It Works Early |
|---|---|---|
| Personal onboarding (you set up their first listing) | Time only | Removes all listing friction for the people who matter most |
| Waived fees for early sellers | Revenue you don't have yet anyway | Zero marginal cost pre-launch |
| Direct line to you for issues | Time only | Builds trust no automated system can replicate at this stage |
| Featured placement / first-mover visibility | Free, you control placement | Reward for taking the early risk |

>  **Tip:** Promising "we'll have lots of buyers soon" is a claim you can't back up yet. Promising concrete, deliverable-today value (no fees, your direct help) is a claim you can keep.

## Common Mistake: Building the Full Seller Dashboard First

> ️ Seller analytics, payout history, bulk listing tools, performance graphs — none of this matters when you have three sellers you personally know. Build the dashboard once sellers ask for it, not because professional marketplaces have one. Every hour here is an hour not spent getting your first transaction to happen.

## AI Prompt: Pressure-Testing Your Seller Onboarding

```
I'm building a personal-scale marketplace for [your niche].
I have zero buyers and zero sellers right now — this is a true cold start.

Seller persona: [your answer]
What they do instead today: [your answer]
Real cost of listing for them: [your answer]
What makes them list once and never return: [your answer]
How they expect to get paid: [your answer]

Act as a skeptical seller who has been burned by joining "promising"
early-stage platforms before. Specifically:
1. What part of this onboarding still asks too much before delivering
   any value back?
2. Given I have zero buyers yet, what's the most credible reason I
   could give this person to list anyway?
3. What's the single biggest reason a seller like this churns after
   their first listing, and how would I detect that early?

Be specific to my niche, not generic marketplace advice.
```

One prompt, fully specified — avoid running this multiple times with vague answers. Fill in real specifics first.

## Validating Before You Build

- Personally recruit 3-5 real sellers by hand (DM, ask people you know) — don't build a seller signup flow yet
- List their item/service for them yourself, manually — note every question you had to ask them that a form should've captured
- Ask directly: "What would make you stop using this after one listing?" — write down the literal answer, don't paraphrase it into something more flattering

---

## What You Should Walk Away With

1. A one-paragraph seller persona
2. The friction map: which fields are required vs. deferred, and why
3. Your cold-start offer — the concrete thing you give early sellers instead of "scale"
4. At least 3 real seller conversations, not assumptions

Cross-check this against your Buyer Journey module: your seller's "cost of listing" and your buyer's "minimum credibility signal" need to line up. If buyers need detailed photos to trust a listing but your seller friction model treats photos as optional, that's a contradiction to resolve now — not after you've built the listing form.
