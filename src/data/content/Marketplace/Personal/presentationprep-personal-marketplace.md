# Presentation Prep

You've built something with real mechanics behind it — supply, demand, trust, liquidity data. Presenting it well, whether to friends, a small local community, potential collaborators, or just documenting it for yourself, means translating that system into a story someone can grasp in minutes, not the months it took you to build it.

## The Decision You're Actually Making

Not "what should we say about the project." It's: **what's the one thing about this marketplace that makes it more than just another app — and does our story actually lead with that?**

A marketplace's real achievement isn't the UI or the tech stack. It's that you got two strangers to trust each other and complete a transaction through something you built alone. That's the story worth telling.

## Why Marketplaces Need a Different Story Than Other Apps

| A typical app's story | A marketplace's story |
|---|---|
| "I built a tool that does X" | "I built a system where two sides — buyers and sellers — trust each other enough to transact" |
| Impressive because of features | Impressive because of the trust, liquidity, and coordination problem solved |
| Demo shows a feature working | Demo has to show an actual match happening between two sides |

> ** Best Practice:** Lead with the liquidity story, not the feature list. "We went from zero listings to a working marketplace where sellers in [category] reliably sell within days" is a far more compelling opening than "it has listings, messaging, payments, and reviews" — the first shows you solved the hard problem, the second just shows you built software.

## What to Show, In Order

1. **The coordination problem** — why bringing buyers and sellers together here was hard, in plain language
2. **How you solved cold start** — the actual chicken-and-egg strategy from Phase 0, since this is the part most people genuinely don't know how to do
3. **Proof of liquidity** — real numbers or a real transaction, not just a feature tour
4. **One specific trust mechanism** — reviews, verification, dispute handling — the piece you're proudest of
5. **What you learned from real usage** — retention/liquidity insights from this phase, showing this wasn't just built and abandoned

> ** Tip:** The cold-start story is almost always the most interesting part to an audience, because it's the part people assume is impossible for a solo builder — "how did you get the first sellers when there were no buyers yet" is a genuinely good story if you have a real answer.

## Use AI to Sharpen Your Story

**Prompt — Marketplace Story Structure**
```
I built a personal-project marketplace for [describe what's bought/sold]. 
Here's what happened:
[describe your cold-start approach, current liquidity in your best 
category, and one specific trust/safety decision you made]

Help me structure a presentation that leads with the coordination 
problem (getting two sides to trust each other) rather than a feature 
list. Suggest what to cut if I only have 3 minutes to tell this story.
```

> ** Token Efficiency:** Include your actual cold-start story and real numbers, not a hypothetical — a presentation built on your specific real decisions is more credible and easier for AI to help sharpen than one built on generic marketplace concepts.

## Validate Before You Present

- The opening leads with the trust/liquidity problem, not a feature list
- You have one real, specific number (sell-through rate, transaction count) to cite, not just "it works"
- The cold-start story is included — it's usually the most memorable part
- You can explain your one proudest trust/safety decision in one or two sentences

> **️ Warning:** Avoid presenting a marketplace with zero real transactions as if it already has meaningful liquidity. If you're pre-liquidity, tell that story honestly — "here's how I'm solving cold start" is still a genuinely interesting narrative, and overstating traction is more likely to be caught out in questions than honesty is to disappoint.

## Common Mistakes

- Opening with tech stack or feature list instead of the coordination problem
- No real numbers cited — only vague claims like "it's working well"
- Skipping the cold-start story, which is usually the most distinctive part of a marketplace's journey
- Presenting theoretical liquidity as if it were proven, when it isn't yet

## Quick Reference

| Lead with | Include | Skip |
|---|---|---|
| The trust/coordination problem | One real liquidity number | Full feature walkthrough |
| Cold-start strategy | The proudest trust/safety decision | Complete tech stack details |
| A real transaction or match | What you learned from real usage | Speculative future roadmap in depth |
